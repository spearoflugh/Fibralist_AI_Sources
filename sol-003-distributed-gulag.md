I am giving a course on distributed programming/network programming for engineers. It is hard to know what to say in such courses because the subject is vast: it can go from technical details to very general software architecture design principles. Moreover I have the feeling that I could talk about some issues for hours that it won’t make its way through the brain of students. There are things that you have to live through to learn and just hearing about theoretical remarks can’t make it (though they can help later to reinterpret everyone’s own experience). Thinking about this course I have found it remarkable how many ideas can help to understand how our society is evolving in front of our eyes. It is similar to the re-foundation of society following the industrial revolution. Just take a look back at how institutions evolved and it is clear that everything started to look like a factory: from school to armies. We are in the midst of a shift that is at least of this scale - in my opinion it is even more profound. We are moving towards a digital society at an accelerated pace. There were two moments where things changed. The first one took place during the course of the 2000’s: everyone eventually had access to internet. The second moment started in the 2010’s, particularly following the introduction of the smartphone: everyone got on the network 24/7. And those are not allegorical terms: everyone means everyone on earth and 24/7 really means at any time of the day or night (with repercussions on sleep etc.). More than just having access to computing power and information, it is the network effect that acted the most on the society. Just like factories required a lot of manpower, and symmetrically freed manpower from agriculture through mechanization, led to development of cities, large condos complex etc. The same thing is occurring with the distributization of the world: moving towards a society that is more distributed by the day. I already wrote about that in this post: Here I am going to address how some concepts from the distributed system field can be of help to better understand the transformation of our society. Distributization of the world Distributed systems were not really thought beforehand. Unlike computers, for which a deep theory was developed before the artifacts even existed, it all started incrementally. Basically people just established a line of communication between two computers and tinkered around. The distinctive feature of distributed systems is that you are hardly pressed to pinpoint where, and what, they are. If you try to explain what is a program on a computer, it is easy: it is a way to control the transformation of the content of the memory of the computer. Instructions to do so are written in a file that you can print, read or edit. A distributed system is an evanescent thing: actually it made of the interactions between many computers, it is not one program nor the sum of all programs involved. It is more like a dance between systems. Take the example of a very large distributed system that you are using on a daily basis: the electronic mail. If you think at what is, from a fundamental perspective, the “email” then it is not clear. There is not a big computer for the world that manage the emails. It is the collection of all mail servers - I can set up a new server in an afternoon - plus the possibility for those servers to interact. They can use zillions of technologies to do so, the servers may run on different operating systems, or not work etc. So you cannot say “email is this computer”, “email is this program (or even protocol)”, nor “email is this set of wires and routers”, it doesn’t work either. Somehow it is the sum of all these technological artifact *nicely working together* that we call “email”. The fact that we have access to networks - something like instantaneous global communication capabilities - has changed our society in depth. Sometimes this is called “ Uberisation of society ”. And it is true that the idea of Uber is to adapt a distributed mindset to solve a problem: you no longer need a fleet of taxi, you can use the cars of privates that will synchronize with customers through a distributed system: the driver and the passengers have an app that communicates with Uber’s servers that arrange the meeting, the price of the lift etc. Notice that Uber’s work doesn’t involve cars nor managing employees. In some sense it is just the implementation of a protocol allowing customers and service providers to agree. As is it the case for the email, it is hard to pinpoint what Uber is precisely. The most interesting and difficult phenomenons to study in distributed systems are how to recover from errors. Indeed due to the fact you don’t control the environment there will be errors to deal with even if you write a code that has no bug - a thing that does not exist in the first place - and is locally correct. On top of that the rate at which people join the system has non trivial implications for the whole system. Think traffic jam: @sophie.stoeger #westcampus #cruisecar #selfdrivingcars #austin Tiktok failed to load. Using this kind of lenses can be useful to determine how to identify, and hopefully to react, to societal issues that are novel. Partition Issues A major class of issues to solve with relation to distributed systems are about the choice of strategies on how to address the partitioning of the networks. As the system is working it will happen that some nodes are going to crash or become unreachable for a gazillions of reasons. It may “cut” the system into subpart that are unable to communicate with one another anymore. The problem is that the isolated parts of the system will evolve on their own. They may eventually reach incompatible states and when connectivity will be restored it is going to be a nightmare to reach consensus again. You have recognize the problem of tribalism in a digital society. Our political institutions were built under the assumption that “we the people”… Is this assumption still valid today? Social media have transformed the media landscape much deeper than usually thought. Those transformations have nth order effects on whole of our society. For instance nowadays people are mainly informed via their social media accounts. What they see of the world on social media is partly under their control (the list of accounts they are following). It is clearly in the control of the platform. At the end of the day it is the Twitter algorithm that is building my feed. Incentives being what they are tribalism is incentivized on social media platforms. The aim of platforms is to tune things so that you stay on the platform as long as possible, because they sell eyeballs time to sponsors. And it turns out that creating a bubble of confirmation bias works really great. This is well known for years. The unprecedented polarization of the political landscape is just one aftermath of this phenomenon. It appears like the last evolution of Twitter/X makes this even more pronounced: the fact that feed is delivered by small packets and that I am only able to see in this feed (that is without checking manually) only a fraction of the publications done by the account I follow show that we have reached the finest granularity level - the individual. It makes harder to build conversations that are shared amongst many participants because other people don’t see the replies in their feeds. So more and more what was a way to publish an open letter to the world is becoming a monologue or an equivalent of a private dialogue done publicly. Because whatever y ou will say on social media will be used against you . The question is: how do you address the issue of partition in distributed system? Well there is no bullet proof answers. In fact there is this CAP theorem stating that any distributed system can only provide two of the following three guaranties: Consistency : Every read receives the most recent write or an error. Availability: Every request receives a (non-error) response, without the guarantee that it contains the most recent write. Partition tolerance : The system continues to operate despite an arbitrary number of messages being dropped (or delayed) by the network between nodes. So every “solution” is in fact a trade off in the very literal sense. You have to drop a guaranty. It looks like that we have opted for 2 and 3. Things continue to work even when some non negligible part of the population is incapable to define “what is a woman” anymore. The implication is that we have lost consistency. And it will not come as a surprise to anyone following the news. The narrative spins and shifts are completely out of control. Official representatives can state one thing and its opposite just a day apart, sometimes even in a single sentence. We should have known, only relying on 2 and 3 meant that we gave up on 1. Node Issues Another typical class of problem is about individual nodes. What happens to the system when nodes starts to behave in a rogue way. Either the nodes are no longer reachable (because of local crash or network issues) or they are reachable but they don’t behave as expected (because standard have changed and the node did not update, or it was hacked or fill in the blanks). I don’t know why but the image of president Biden just popped up in my mind. More generally the “node failures” find their counterpart with many shapes and forms: Aftermath of cancel culture : don’t be fooled by the Twitter files. Cancel culture is alive and kicking. I am not going to give example: just read the news and you are going to find the “exemple du jour”. Opioid crisis : I don’t recall times of my existence when things looked so horrific on this front. The streets of Philadelphia, and countless other cities, are nothing short of zombie movies of the 70s. So many lives have been wasted, it is heart wrenching. Drug involved overdose death was around 100K. The figure was 20K in 1999 (see NIH numbers ). This is in par with the number of US death during WW1 or the Korean War . It is increasing and just for a single year. Financial breakdown of individuals : inflation is here for anyone having eyes to see, foreclosures are rising again and more generally the economic prospects of individuals look more and more restraint etc. “Working overtime hours for bullshit pay” may come to mind. Typical solutions in distributed systems to recover from failing nodes are of two schools: 1- either reset to a previously stored state that is known to be consistent or 2- use replication to catch up. But life is not a video game. You don’t have “insert coins to continue” options. Even the reset option is hard to implement. This is the idea of redemption. If it is talked about for thousands of pages in the gospels it is precisely because it is not easy to implement. It looks like we have entered an impasse but what can we do? We have to hope in redemption now more than ever. The fact that internet never forgets makes it even harder: there will always someone somewhere practicing necromancy that will unearth one thing you said one night while drunk. It will be used as a proof of the fact that you are guilty, forever. Things look grim on this front. Byzantine Problems Every node problem that is not a crash (or impossibility to connect with a node) is classified as a Byzantin problem. It comes from a seminal research paper by L. Lamport on how to settle, from a theoretical point of view, the question about the survivability of a distributed system while nodes are failing to do their jobs: “ The Byzantine General Problem ”. I have long felt that, because it was posed as a cute problem about philosophers seated around a table, Dijkstra’s dining philosopher’s problem received much more attention than it deserves. (For example, it has probably received more attention in the theory community than the readers/writers problem, which illustrates the same principles and has much more practical importance.) I believed that the problem introduced in [41] was very important and deserved the attention of computer scientists. The popularity of the dining philosophers problem taught me that the best way to attract attention to a problem is to present it in terms of a story. There is a problem in distributed computing that is sometimes called the Chinese Generals Problem, in which two generals have to come to a common agreement on whether to attack or retreat, but can communicate only by sending messengers who might never arrive. I stole the idea of the generals and posed the problem in terms of a group of generals, some of whom may be traitors, who have to reach a common decision. I wanted to assign the generals a nationality that would not offend any readers. At the time, Albania was a completely closed society, and I felt it unlikely that there would be any Albanians around to object, so the original title of this paper was The Albanian Generals Problem. Jack Goldberg was smart enough to realize that there were Albanians in the world outside Albania, and Albania might not always be a black hole, so he suggested that I find another name. The obviously more appropriate Byzantine generals then occurred to me. There is no universal solution. In facts depending on the hypotheses many strategies can be proposed to address Byzantine problems. As a matter of fact Bitcoin is just one particular solution to a Byzantine problem: how do you insure that the ledger recording who sent what to who remains consistent? It is still an active area of research in computer science. There is an idea that emerges through every solutions though: there is no free lunch. You have to pay somewhere to have a system that continue to work while facing rogue actors. It is the idea of “ proof of work ” in the Bitcoin network for instance. So what does it implies in societal terms? It means that thing that used to work more or less without friction in centralized society (where orders come top down) won’t work as easily in a distributed society. There is a price to be paid. My hunch is that many problems that we are encountering today comes from the fact that we are refusing to pay this fee because it used to be free, and it worked. This can be witnessed in the media sphere. We used to trust MSM for which the fee was low and very well identified. Nowadays we are gathering information through social media, it bears many interesting properties but we are not ready to adjust. Maybe one reason is that we don’t know yet how to organize things in this new environment. For instance we are flooded by robots on X selling OnlyFans advertisement under cover of fake accounts: those are clearly “rogue nodes” of the “Byzantine problem”. Clearly X and Facebook/Instagram are using different strategies to mitigate those issues. It is too early to know what will emerge but things cannot stay like that. Eventual Consistency Here is not the place to give a course on the different models of consistency that are used to set up distributed systems. Depending on what the system is supposed to do you can have several notions of consistency. Some systems may handle inconsistencies better than others - so they are less strict on the constraint they pose on the programmer. The most relaxed model is called “Eventual consistency”. By the way … eventual consistency is only used with humans because only humans are able to tolerate such a level of inconsistency and still continue to work… That is why it is used on social media (X is based on an eventual consistency model ). The idea of eventual consistency is that “[eventual consistency] informally guarantees that, if no new updates are made to a given data item, eventually all accesses to that item will return the last updated value.” (see the wikipedia article ). Something like a computer scientist version of “They lived happily ever after the last update”. But life goes on, it is what it does, doesn’t it? So there will never be “a last update”. Distributed system science suggests that living in a not-so-coherent society is here to stay. You cannot hope to have magically a return to global consistency (aka "retvrn” mythology). Deal with it.
