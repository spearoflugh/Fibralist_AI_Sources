    <concept name="Epistemic Anorexia — The Sin of Radical Abstinence">
      In a world of scarcity, gluttony (consuming too much) is the cardinal sin. In a world
      of data overabundance, the inverse sin emerges: radical abstinence — discarding too
      much data rather than processing it. Cancel culture is partly this phenomenon: instinct
      tells people to discard rather than integrate information that doesn't fit existing
      categories. "Symmetrically to gluttony (excess in a world of rarity) there might be
      a new sin to invent: to discard too much data (fasting in a world of oversupply) is a
      sign of laziness and should not be encouraged." The anorexia parallel: "anapliroforia"
      (information anorexia) is the proposed neologism. The corpus's epistemological position:
      neither gluttony (undiscriminating consumption) nor anorexia (reflexive rejection) but
      active calibration.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Labelling as Coarse-Grain Thinking — Guilt by Association">
      Hashtag culture is the most visible symptom of data-overload-driven coarse thinking:
      labelling is efficient for retrieval but catastrophic for nuance. "Being labelled red
      or blue on a specific issue immediately comes with a set of ideas" that have no logical
      connection to the original position (2nd Amendment → presumed positions on abortion,
      death penalty, immigration). These "natural tribality reflexes are on steroids in our
      digitized culture." The structural mechanism: data overload → labelling as cognitive
      shortcut → guilt by association → elimination of nuance → polarization. The SEO
      industry as the professionalization of labelling: "the habit of adding keywords is
      natural but in this context it becomes even more pronounced" and "sneaks into your
      thinking process."
    </concept>
    <concept name="Data Overlords — The Invisible Organization of Knowledge">
      The externalization of memory from oral (brains) to written (libraries, hierarchical
      organization) to digital (search engines, algorithms, hashtags) represents not a
      disappearance of organization but a displacement: the work of organizing data moves
      from visible (scribes, librarians, editors) to invisible (PageRank, recommendation
      algorithms). "The work of organizing data does not disappear because it is rendered
      frictionless from the user point of view." The semantic web (RDF, SPARQL) promised
      to make this explicit — it has not delivered. The current transition: half the work
      is done manually (user hashtags), half by invisible algorithms. The user believes
      they are making choices; the algorithm is making most of them.

<content>
The widespread use of research engines and AI has shaped the world, more precisely the way we represent the world in our minds. We think of it in hashtags and sort feelings and ideas into categories dutifully labeled. It is a very poor thinking hygiene and leads to a new kind of sin that is the inverse of gluttony.

## Data overload

The technologies used to transmit and acquire information shape our view of the world. Until recently, say around 10 years ago, we were in a culture primarily centered around written text where there was little room for interactive knowledge. Books represented the authority in terms of validity of knowledge. The idea was that books were edited thoroughly before publication: printing a physical book is costly (ink, paper, distribution of atoms around the globe). In a digitized culture only electrons need to be moved around — besides being lighter, once a cable has been installed the marginal cost of using it tends to 0. So broadcasting text is essentially free. This feature allows you to correct errors much more easily. Therefore the need for a very hard editorial process goes down.

There is another dimension in which new information technologies interact with culture-building. We publish so much new information every day that we face a problem rather unknown to humankind: data overload. There is simply too much data produced to be processed by humans. That is why search engines and techniques are paramount.

## Data Overlords

The ability to find its way inside libraries replaced, in written cultures, the memory techniques used in oral cultures. Socrates was very critical against written culture — the externalization of memory into books was one of his fiercest points (along with the lack of interaction with the text). The access techniques for information retrieval have morphed again with technological innovation. The very concept of a well-organized library may sound strange to a digital native.

The work of organizing data does not disappear because it is rendered frictionless from the user point of view. We have facilities that are invisible — like the PageRank algorithm — in order to measure the significance of information in a network. The hashtag technology is a more visible example: a very raw solution to add meta-information to published data in order to help retrieval (keywords).

So we find ourselves in a transition in which half of the work is done manually. This is the user who adds hashtags. It has been transformed into a whole industry: a large part of "clickbait" activity is to be well referenced by search engines. I suspect that it sneaks into your thinking process: the habit of adding keywords becomes even more pronounced.

## Quantity and superficiality

It is bad thinking hygiene because labelling is a coarse-grain way of thinking. The data overload nudges people to find ways to save on data processing time. Labelling leads to "guilt by association." The typical result can be really bad for the clarity of discussion because there are hidden implied presuppositions. If you look at the American political landscape it appears divided at an unprecedented point. The labelling process is not the only factor but it is a strong one. Being labelled red or blue on a specific issue immediately comes with a set of ideas. Typically if you are a supporter of the 2nd amendment, then presumably one could derive a whole list of other ideas you are supposed to have that have no logical bounds with this specific issue. For instance what are your positions on pro-life/pro-choice, death penalty, drug legalization, immigration policy etc. These natural tribality reflexes are on steroids in our digitized culture.

It can be the case that cancel culture is also in part due to this overload of data. Instinctively people try to discard new data instead of gathering as much as possible, as it was always the case historically. Symmetrically to gluttony (which is excess in a world of rarity) there might be a new sin to invent: to discard too much data (which is fasting in a world of oversupply) is a sign of laziness and should not be encouraged. The parallel with anorexia (anapliroforia?) is not fortuitous.

Finding the right balance between taking into account any piece of data and downright rejecting all data that do not meet our confirmation biases is a work to be done. We now have to do choices that were never choices before. We need to arbitrate in the right way and it is not clear yet how to do it. The only thing I know is that radical abstinence is not the way.
