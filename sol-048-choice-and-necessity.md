    <concept name="The Topological Inversion of Choice and Necessity">
      The central thesis of this article is a direct consequence of the Topological
      Revolution (sol-002): choice and necessity have been swapped. The pre-digital
      default — you were constrained by your geographical neighbors (necessity) but
      could not choose your cultural environment (no real freedom to move) — has
      inverted. Now: you can choose your virtual neighbors freely (narrative geography)
      but you have lost the ability to refuse the composition of your physical
      neighborhood (immigration, digital legislation, monetary policy decided without
      democratic mandate). "Choice and necessity have been swapped for one another."
      The paradox: more apparent freedom (choose your timeline, your community, your
      country of residence) produces more real constraint (the subjects that matter most
      are decided without you).
    </concept>
    <concept name="The One-Way Mirror of Social Media">
      The structural illusion of democratic participation via social media: I can reply
      to an Emmanuel Macron post — technically identical to how he posts. But the
      communication is asymmetric: his DHL-4 account receives thousands of replies that
      he cannot process; my DHL-1.3 reply is effectively inaudible. The paradox: while
      looking more elitist, pre-digital institutions were less cut from reality than
      today's digital elites — because the feedback loops (letters, audiences, meetings)
      were fewer but more substantive. Today's elite believes it is interactive (posting
      on X) while being structurally isolated by scale. Both citizen and elite have lost
      freedom — both respond to necessities imposed by the technology, not to each other.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Policy Without Mandate — The Democratic Void">
      Certain high-impact policy domains — immigration levels, digital ID legislation,
      surveillance architecture — are consistently absent from electoral campaigns while
      being massively implemented by executive decree or regulatory fiat. The mechanism:
      the political cost of being explicit is too high in a social media environment where
      Terminal Narratives (sol-024d) dominate. Better to avoid the subject entirely than
      to face the strange attractor. The result: voters choose between candidates on
      the issues that are named, while the real structural changes occur in the unnamed
      space. "No matter who you vote for, you get John McCain" (T. Woods). This is
      Adiabatic Politics (sol-022) applied to the democratic mandate: the system appears
      closed to citizen input precisely because the input channels have multiplied.
    </concept>

There were not a lot of options in the 19th century England when the family finances were shaky and you had five daughters. Your whole life turned around how to marry them efficiently. Pride and Prejudice is a world renown novel, and later movie, exploring this scenario. Today, on a world stage, we are facing another scenario where external factors have a direct impact into our daily lives. During all of human history we had no choice but to live with our geographical neighbors. Nowadays, thanks to the combination of information technologies and modern transportation systems, it is no longer the case. You can choose who are your neighbors based on a virtual geography: the narratives that you like for instance. Try looking at the news on BlueSky versus X for instance. The liberation of the last Israeli's hostages is telling, you are going to watch two totally different movies. On the other hand, with a world so thoroughly connected, you would have hard times to find discussions about digital ID, chat control or migration policies for instance. We see the end result, for instance a vote, or the rise of immigrants, but those subjects are never explicitly discussed during electoral campaigns. In the 70s we had the choice of the policies, at least that was the promise of democracy, but not of our neighbors. Things changed. This is at the heart of what I call "The Topological Revolution."

This inversion is an unexpected aftermath of the topological revolution. It is the subject of this piece: choice and necessity have been swapped for one another.

## Multiple Choices Democracies

Travel was hard. That is why the concept of a passport is a new thing. The modern version only dates back to the end of the first world war. Before, passports were very limited in their scope and applications. But with the development of travel, people started to move around and were able to make new choices: where am I going to spend my life? was never a choice before that. Of course there were migrations, colonization and co. but they were about one way trips. Worldwide trips are now available to everyone, plus it is not going to take weeks to reach your destination but hours. You can literally switch continents for the week-end on a regular basis.

But there is a flip side to this choice. People welcoming you at the other side of the earth have lost their ability to define who they are. At least it appears to be so in the west. Since 20 years the level of immigration has reached unseen levels. In Canada, admittedly an extreme case, the numbers show dramatic increases. Not a single presidential candidate ever ran on this issue. It was done by the equivalent of executive orders and the policy was never discussed or openly chosen.

So you have both the choice — to move out — and the absence of choice — you can't refuse the massive influx of immigrants — at the same time in our western democracies. Maybe it is because before no one ever really had the choice to move, or at great cost, that today we are not even considering it to be worthy of discussion? I don't really know. What I do know is that immigration is only one subject on which things go one way in the absence of political discussion. I mentioned digital ID, various digital legislation (anonymity, the ban of such and such social media platform, age control etc.). On the international there is this famous quote "no matter who you vote for you get John McCain" by Th. Woods.

Trump's administration promised explicitly to break this cycle. During the 2024 campaign he stated that releasing the Epstein file would be a way to destroy the deep state/drain the swamp. We all know what happened (nothing was released to this day). So in this case the citizen got the inverse of what they explicitly voted for. Even if Trump's administration seems dedicated to act on the immigration, it is of a separate concern. My point is to say that not only some very important subjects are ignored in the public discussion, sometimes the complete opposite of what has been decided by the voters is done. Is it a new thing? How come is it possible in an era where so many communications means are available?

## The One-Way mirror

Social media have at least made the public opinion more available than before. You could stand up on a soap box in your favorite square, there were very few chances that your voice would ever reach the elites. Surely there is a part of this modern sentiment of misalignment that comes from this.

I think that there is more to the story. And the one-way mirror property of social media might be an important part of it. The idea is that though you have the intuitive impression that communication is symmetrical, direct and simple on social media — after all I can reply to an X post of Emmanuel Macron — it is not true. Emmanuel Macron has 10.2 million followers and typically one of his posts elicits thousands of replies. Even if he were in charge of his account, like JD Vance is, he would not be able to read and reply as easily as I have replied to some of his posts. Surely for both accounts the discussion on the social media platform takes the same form: write a tweet and press button "reply". From a technical point of view there is no difference. Though there are many from each other point of view. Now, a few decades back the head of state, and the managerial class in general, was aware of the difference between a speech and a citizen telling him few words. The paradox being that while looking more elitist the old institutions were less cut from the world than today. Because if the average citizen might see himself as an influencer, those technologies have an effect on the people in charge too. They might see themselves as influencers too. Or at least they might think that they have to play the game and appear as such. In this regard Trump and his administration have worked a lot. The ongoing meme war between the administration and the Democrats is a one of a kind show. Thus it appears that somehow, the elite class, is also subjected to necessity. The overall picture is strange: both the citizen and the institutional elites have more capacities, thanks to technology to do things, more opportunities to be heard, but in fact it turns out that both have lost their freedom and are responding to necessities that emanate from those same technologies. Or are things not clear yet?
