This post is a follow up to an earlier post on Social Media Topology. I was raised in a family of physicians. Both my parents are physicians as well as other members of my close relatives. I was constantly reading medical literature since I was able to read. One of the thing that stroke me was this image of how the relative parts of our body are mapped to the brain. This is called the proprioception. If we draw a picture of a human such that body parts have a size that are proportionate with how much of the brain they use, we obtain a distorted homunculus where hands, mouth and face dominate overwhelmingly.

Obviously we talk a lot and manipulate things with our hands. Your brain doesn't spend a lot of energy controlling what goes on with your belly or your feet.

Now with this image in mind compare it to images of influencers that you find on social media. Body parts appears as caricatures of standard human beings. This is most noticeable (at least for me) for women influencers, but also occur with men. In such examples, say Instagram influencer, the parts that are enhanced and emphasized (with Photoshop/AI edition) are basic sexual cues: butt, breasts, hips waist ratio, and lips have an exaggerated size. There is nothing new here (remember the paleolithic Venus?). What has changed is that we are using surgical procedures and computer filters to make real the importance of what our brain attaches to the sexual clues. This exaggeration of our impulses can be seen in many domains. Some of them are abstract and cannot be visually seen. For instance the polarization of politics appears to be another instance of the same phenomenon. Tribal social wars too: they come with their own memes and pictograms that social media users stick to their profile. And as time goes on the global discussion turns into a carnival.

## The clown shift

I don't know if the recent trend of Peterson Twitter posts is linked but it appears to be so. For a few weeks now the famous Canadian psychologist has posted a lot of pictures of grotesque clowns as the only comment as replies or quote tweets to woke tweets. I cannot read minds but the intent and the trend are clear enough. This is a particularly vivid illustration of what I am talking about here. I am inclined to think that it was done on purpose unlike many other instances.

Another Twitter example, though on a different dimension, is the evolution of the account of Aella on Twitter. As time went on the polls were getting weirder and weirder. What was interesting edge became a caricature of itself.

It looks like there is a social media equivalent of the cosmological red shift. As internet and social media grow bigger, either be it in terms of number users or for each user separately, we all (I don't exclude myself from this effect) are shifting to caricature of ourselves online. Of course one part is due to the audience capture aspect, Nikocado Avocado being the poster child of this particular effect, but there is more.

## Chesterton's Fence

My thesis is that in a virtual space you never encounter real limits. You can fly like superman with your Apple virtual reality set, at least you can dream that you can fly. The imagination and abilities of the programmers are the limit. It means that those limits are not outside of ourselves. There is here a fundamental difference with reality. Everyone who has tried to use his arms as a bird uses its wings know it.

Traditionally taboos, moral principles, mythologies (Icarus, Prometheus etc.) and religious practices in general are the fences used to constraint our minds. If you let yourself mentally wandering around without paying attention to the outside world you may end up like Theodore Kaczynski: locked in a double jail. A mental jail in which you are trapped by your own thoughts and a real jail because going crazy is not something that will make you go far in society. He died last week inside a penitentiary room.

Technology has progressed far much than wisdom. We can do much more than we understand. It is especially true in the virtual world. Using the superpower of modern communication and computation is no longer limited to specialists (who also have turned crazy anyway as the dire state of academia shows) but available to anyone. We have started to think that old traditions were no longer valid because we could call a surgeon and have a "gender affirming surgery" — whatever that means — and that having a healthy lifestyle was important but Instagram filters and body positivity movement grew at the same pace: who cares that you are obese, you are just one click away from looking like a top model on social media, which is increasingly the only way people are going to see you anyway.

Any resemblance to the story of the Chesterton's Fence is not purely coincidental.
