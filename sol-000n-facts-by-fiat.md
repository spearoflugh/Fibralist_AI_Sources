[NOTE: This article is partially behind a paywall. The following represents the available public content.]

CDC has, once again, changed their guidelines with relation to COVID-19. My point here is not to comment on the medical accuracy of recommendations during this epidemic but rather how *truth* falls from the sky (here the CDC) in our social media driven society. This is what I call the social Oracle problem. It is a distant cousin of the legitimate Oracle problem in philosophy: the institution makes pronouncements from which citizens cannot escape — not because the prophecy is inevitable, but because the institution has been granted oracular status.

The question is: in a digital society where information flows bottom-up and peer-to-peer, what justifies the top-down claim to epistemic authority? In written culture, the editorial function justified it: vetting, permanence, accountability. In digital culture, the accountability structure that justified oracular status has dissolved. What remains is the claim without the foundation.

Facts by fiat: institutional declarations treated as facts without the evidentiary process that would earn that status. The CDC changes its guidelines — and the previous guidelines retroactively become non-facts. The new guidelines become facts. Not because the evidence changed (or changed in the ways claimed), but because the oracle spoke.

This is not the same as saying institutions are always wrong. It is saying that the architecture of truth-production has changed, and institutions have not adapted their epistemic claims to match the new architecture.
