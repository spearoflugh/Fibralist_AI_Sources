    The raison d’être of Fibralism is to shift the standpoint on politics in order to preserve culture. Why? Because culture is a wealth that cannot be produced by reason alone. Since the time filter cannot be replaced by simulation, the accumulation of wisdom we call culture is the most precious good available to humans. Any material object can be copied, repaired, or replaced. The same can be said of money and finance. But culture cannot be invented by an individual, nor can it be created within a single generation, even by an arbitrarily large number of people. Culture is therefore simultaneously a human creation and something that transcends the mere existence of any individual. It is in this sense that I call it sesqui-theology. Sesqui means “one and a half” in Latin. It is more than mere theology in the sense that it remains accessible from a human standpoint: one can experience a culture, be shaped by it, and recognize its depth without any prior metaphysical commitment. Yet it is also more than a purely rational construct — its most essential dimension, the thread woven across generations, exceeds what any single mind can grasp or verify. The leap required is not faith in a personal God, but something humbler and harder to refuse: the acknowledgment that we received something we did not make, and that we owe something to those who have not yet arrived.

Culture is also irreducibly social. Would culture mean anything on a desert island? At best it would collapse into self-discipline — habits without an audience, a language without a listener. This reveals something essential: culture is fundamentally an address to the other, including the temporal other — the generations that precede and follow.

It therefore makes sense to understand Fibralism as a polity organized around a sesqui-theonomy. In a Fibrization there is no state religion, but a sesqui-theological norm shared by the Fibrizens. The political question becomes: what are the means to make culture thrive? It is not about creating culture, but about building the conditions for a culture to survive in a digital era where everything changes at the speed of a click.

One can contemplate this problematic along two dimensions: the normative and the temporal. They raise an immediate practical question: how does a sesqui-theonomy manifest itself? A norm that lives only in texts and declarations is already dead — it would ultimately be nothing but a spectre of written culture. Culture, whether digital or not, must be embodied: in rituals, in shared space, in the rhythm of collective life. I will therefore examine two angles: first, the necessity of tangible expression within a Fibrization; second, the specific challenge that the temporal dimension poses in a digital era where the very notion of continuity is under assault.
Benjamin Constant

The Liberty of the Fibrizen

Democratic life revolves around political campaigns and votes. For Benjamin Constant, Ancient Liberty was the right to rule — the liberty of the Agora, of collective self-governance. He opposed it to Modern Liberty: the right to be left alone, the liberty of leisure and individual independence. Yet Constant warned that if citizens stopped engaging with public life entirely, they would eventually forfeit the very privacy they cherished. It looks as though our dying written culture has reached peak Constant.

What form, then, shall liberty take in a Fibralist society? The answer, I would argue, lies in participation in the sesqui-theological norm. This means that certain practical dimensions of life in common are not left to individual discretion — they are enacted by the Fibrizen as an expression of belonging. This is not the absence of liberty but a different conception of it: not the freedom from obligation, but the freedom that comes through a shared form — much as a musician is not diminished by mastering counterpoint, but liberated by it.

The most immediate examples are language and defense. Both are non-negotiable dimensions of any Fibrization: one binds the living to one another and to the dead; the other ensures that the thread is not cut by force.

Hence we see a third form of liberty. To the negative liberty, and the expressive liberty, I am adding the bequeathing liberty. The idea being that you are the recipient of a culture that you are going to transmit but not as a static object passed from hand to hand, but as a living continuity, a social environment capable of thriving beyond your own existence. You are, in this sense, less the owner of a culture than its temporary steward. What you owe to those who have not yet arrived is not a perfect inheritance, but a resilient one — a keel, not a monument.

This may seem abstract. What does it translate to in practice? At the individual level, bequeathing liberty means inhabiting a culture actively rather than consuming it passively — contributing to its continuity rather than merely benefiting from it. At the collective level, it requires what no liberal polity has seriously attempted: the deliberate structuring of shared time. Something like the architecture of encounter. Let’s talk about the Calendar.
Circular and linear calendars

Calendars

I will focus on my own culture in this section, though similar discussions could be conducted for other Fibers.

The role of the calendar is to serve as a map in the temporal dimension. Just as borders define the limits of the traditional nation-state, calendars are essential to a Fibrization. Why the plural? Because there are both linear and circular calendars. The linear calendar is linked to the idea of progress and the arc of a personal life. The circular calendar concerns the rhythm of seasons: the length of days, astronomical cycles, and everything upon which agriculture depends.

Consider the sacraments: there is one for each threshold of life — birth, adulthood, marriage, death. Likewise, the liturgical calendar was superimposed upon pagan calendars, largely following astronomical events such as solstices and equinoxes. A Western Fiber should draw inspiration from both calendars to build its own version. The key difference, however, is that special days would not be left as holidays — occasions for private leisure — but dedicated to mandatory collective events. This is not an arbitrary imposition: it follows directly from the logic of bequeathing liberty. A culture that is never enacted together is a culture that is slowly forgotten together. The mandatory aspect can be enforced through many means, from social pressure to more formal mechanisms; the details belong to the technopraxis of each Fiber, not to this foundational sketch.

On top of these two calendars, a third should be conceived with a longer span — one dedicated to the sequence of generations. The Olympic Games, held every four years, might be seen as a primitive instance of such multi-generational events: a rhythm that exceeds the individual life without yet spanning the full arc of a civilization. This third calendar should also serve as a synchronization tool for geographically distinct instances of the same Fiber. Just as the Ekecheiria suspended conflict across the entire Hellenic world to make the Games possible, a Fibralist multi-generational calendar would periodically reassert the unity of the Fiber across its dispersed nodes — not through central command, but through shared ritual time. Again, technopraxis is going to be used for a concrete implementation.
