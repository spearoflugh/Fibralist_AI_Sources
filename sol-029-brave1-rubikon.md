    <concept name="Brave1 Market as Distributed Military System">
      Ukraine's Brave1 is not a military procurement catalog — it is a distributed innovation ecosystem for wartime. Units earn points for documented kills (verified by drone footage uploaded to Delta system), spend points to order new weapons delivered to front line within a week. The economic calculation problem of war is solved through market mechanisms rather than central command.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Hayek Catalaxie Applied to War">
      Hayek distinguished spontaneous order (catalaxia — markets, emergent coordination) from artificial orders (armies, hierarchies aimed at specific goals). He thought them incompatible. Brave1 dissolves this distinction: gamification applied to the battlefield creates catallactic incentives within what was previously the paradigmatic artificial order. The market price signal (points per kill type) transmits information about what the battlefield needs without central planning.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Bitcoin Hashrate as Military Model">
      The Bitcoin network automatically adjusts difficulty to maintain one block per X minutes — a distributed KPI system. Brave1 applies this exact logic: the points system is recalibrated as battlefield conditions evolve (new threat types get new point values). Distributed agents optimizing a KPI without central coordination. The first implementation of cryptographic incentive design in warfare.
    </concept>

This is not metaphorical gamification — it is a literal digital marketplace for kinetic outcomes. The implications for how armies are structured and motivated are not yet fully understood.
    </concept>

If you haven't closely followed the Ukraine-Russia war recently, chances are that you haven't the faintest idea of who is the "Brave1" and what is the "Rubikon" (“Rubicon”?) that he has presumably crossed. Most probably you have seen the head of states meetings, Putin here, Meloni there, Macron over there with Trump as the master of ceremonies. But what is happening on the front lines ? It turns out that the digital revolution is also a revolution in the armed confrontations. The revolution behind drones Many have heard about the FPV drones . It is true that nowadays something like 70% of deaths in this war are caused by those electronic insects. In terms of killing efficiency they have supplanted the artillery. Actually war is more complex, artillery is still a major part of wars because, for once, killing is not the only thing that you do at war. Hampering the movement of the opponent is another one and shell rains work very well for that. Nonetheless, the drones represent a major evolution of the battlefield in a digital era: tactics have to be adapted. Even centuries old ditches don't work anymore. When you are trying to get protection from pseudo random shelling, the basic ditch works well. Unless you are unlucky, and the shell lands inside the ditch, it offers a very good protection. But with FPV drones you have an operator directing the payload right on your shoes. It has nothing to do with statistics anymore (you know that if you put X shells per square meter then Z is going to follow). Think sniper with an explosive payload rather than random explosions. By the way, I am sure that you have noticed the name of this substack: the spear of Lugh. It is something like a celtic variation on Mjolnir. You know those weapons that never miss their targets… But the drones are just a technical point. The most visible part of a giant iceberg. It is true that drones sit at the the tip of the spear of new information technologies (communication networks, AI, video, electronics) but they remains just a technical tool. A true and deeper revolution is hiding behind them. If you thought that the gamification of the fight was limited to drones think again. This is where Brave1 market enters the scene. This time it is about the architecture of the army that we are talking about. Incentives and Distributed System Brave1 Market is more than just Amazon shopping for Ukrainian military units. It is a distributed system approach to both logistics and innovation in war time. It is a complete ecosystem turned towards killing the invaders. Instead of launching new apps, Brave1 market proposes new drones and technologies in service of the army. But it is more than just a catalogue of tools. The economic calculation problem (ECP) remains : how much and what kind of tools are needed? How do you know how much resources is optimal for the pursued goal? The best solution is markets. In terms of information theory, market prices are signals sent to autonomous agents that, in fine, arbitrage what is best fit to their goals. In everyday life, autonomous agents are people whom all have different aims. The economic efforts are driven by offer/demand. But how does it translates in war time? From a theoretical point of view they were seen as different. In “ Law, Legislation and Liberty ” Hayek makes the difference between spontaneous order ( catalaxia ) and artificial orders aimed at a specific goal, for which the army is the poster child. Actually the idea of Hayek was that a hierarchical way to convey information had no chance of success in catatalict enterprises. Not that market technologies were incapable of handling artificial orders. The modern word is gamification . And yes it has been adapted to the battlefield. On the Brave1 market you can win points by killing enemies. There is even an official scale that is updated to take into account the evolution of the battelfield . For those working in distributed systems it is not a shock. It is exactly like the automatically setting the hashrate difficulty of the Bitcoing network : you set a goal, say one block every x minutes, and if you are over the goal the network adds an extra 0 to the hash target or removes one 0 if above. Actually it is nothing more than setting incentives right in a network of distributed agents in order to improve a key performance indicator ( KPI ). This gives something like this: The program assigns points for each type of kill: 20 points for damaging and 40 for destroying a tank; up to 50 points for destroying a mobile rocket system, depending on the caliber; and six points for killing an enemy soldier. Soldiers have to download the video footage taken by their drones confirming the kill to the military's Delta communication and situational awareness system. Units will soon be able to use the special digital points they’ve been getting since last year by trading them in for new weapons. A Vampire drone, for example, costs 43 points. The drone, nicknamed Baba Yaga, or witch, is a large multi-rotor drone able to carry a 15-kilogram warhead. The Ukrainian government will pay for the drones that are ordered and will deliver them to the front-line unit within a week. Points for kills: How Ukraine is using video game incentives to slay more Russians - Politico Russia has its own version : the Rubikon unit . Not much is known about this unit but the fact that it is an elite unit that is very independent from the rest of the command chain of the russian army. This is more or less the same story : engineers tinkering drones, loitering ammuniations, and tactics. Amongst the new tactics is this one : they put the drones on the ground waiting for a target to be spotted, saving energy for later. It appears that they are attributing points based on the style of the kill . But you get the idea at this point. Brave1 new war I don’t know where the name “Brave1” comes from. A sensible proposition that comes to mind is that it is a tribute to ‘s “Brave New War” 2007 essay. The focus of this essay was on asymetrical war and terrorism. 20 years laters those ideas have been industrialized and are used in a war between peers. There are many implications at many levels, some of which I have written about in this substack: from the evanescent concept of front line to distributed systems and catalaxia passing by the topological revolution . It is not surprising since war has always been the place where innovations are emerging. The digital revolution is changing *everything* and the pace is accelerating. Institutions are going to be unrecognizable just a decade from now. The battlefields of today are just the avant garde.
