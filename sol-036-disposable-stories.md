  <key_concepts>
    <concept name="Disposable Stories">
      Media narratives built on facts mixed with half-truths, opinions, and political spin, whose function is not to inform but to serve as incentives in a distributed system designed to control the narrative — then be discarded when no longer useful. Three defining features: (1) not falsifiable, remaining vague while linked to major trends; (2) could be ignited almost any time — the trigger is opportunistic, not caused by the event itself; (3) become disposable because of the social media environment, not because of any inherent quality.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Subprime News Crisis">
      By analogy with the 2008 financial crisis: disposable stories are like subprime mortgage products — low-quality narrative assets bundled with higher-quality elements and rated as credible. Like Lehman Brothers, they collapse at the first real bump. The media reality is being changed one disposable story at a time, like a construction worker building a wall with materials that disappear after use.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Program vs Protocol Applied to Narrative">
      Watergate is the polar opposite of a disposable story — it operated like a program: precise, orchestrated, timeline-controlled by named actors (Woodward, Bernstein, Deep Throat). Disposable stories operate like a protocol: no compulsion to follow, open to the environment (your aunt's Facebook post can enter the stream), acting as a road sign not a mandate. The nudging function of the algorithm replaces editorial control.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Ship of Theseus Effect">
      Institutions look structurally intact (same buildings, same voting booths, same courts). But the mental material constituting their worldview has been replaced plank by plank by disposable stories. When the judge reads and applies the law, it is under a paradigm assembled from discarded narratives — like ancient Amazon cities now covered in mold, visible only under laser mapping.
    </concept>

<content>
The internet never forgets they say. Yet the media environment appears to be filled with stories that fulfill a unique purpose and that are later thrown away when they have ceased to be useful. I have kept a record of the "thing of the day" on social media for the whole 2025: a list of the topics that are hot for some news cycles on social media. When I am browsing the list, I find many stories that have sounded dramatic or important at the time. They have all quietly gone away by now. The lack of interest about what they were really about is jarring and reminds the absence of professionalism displayed by the average journalist interviewing a senile POTUS. No push-backs, no follow-up questions, just write "he is sharp as a tack" in your article and everything is going to be fine because, at the end of the day, nobody cares about anything for more than a couple of hours in the social media era.

I am calling those media narratives "disposable stories" and they are the subject of this post. But first things first, let's examine an example of a disposable story. I could have chosen amongst dozens of similar caliber stories but somehow decided to focus on the one about the drones around military bases in Europe at the end of last September. Maybe because it reminds me a similar story that happened last year in New Jersey about drones that, to this day, remains unexplained and for which there is not a single speck of interest in the, social or legacy, media.

The Drones of September

Maybe we have not understood properly the drone phenomenon and they are not robots but living creatures. Just like a flock of birds that seeks out more welcoming places in winter, for some unknown reason, drones gather around airfields in late autumns. In 2024 they were seen in New Jersey and sparked a lot of interest. This year it appears that they have chosen Europe for their gatherings. Maybe due to global warming the phenomenon was a month and a half ahead of schedule this year.

In both cases many airports, civilian and military, have been closed because of the threat that the drones represented. In both cases also many competing explanations were proposed, from the "bad Putin" to the "curious E.T.s" passing by something like "there is nothing to see, people are too dumb and conflate normal flights with spooky things". And in both cases the phenomenon gradually faded away into the background noise of an already completely crazy 2025 year.

I have no final interpretation of both events. As a matter of fact I have stopped to even try to have theories about such stories. My only way to know things is to rely on media because I have no possibility of direct observation. And, in this Cartesian crisis, I have already seen weird shit —this time with my own eyes— being reported in crazy ways in our chaotic media environment. Yet somehow one thing emerges: those stories are a new phenomenon, they are gaining in importance and all this is linked to our new digital culture.

The Subprime News Crisis

The subprime mortgage crisis of 2008 can be summarized as follows: loans were given to people who could not afford them, then those loans were mixed together with other loans of better quality producing a financial product ranked as very good by the rating agencies. Something a like a layered burger in which some layers are made of shit but that is overall rated as a fine quality food. Each disposable story is like one of those financial product. Everything appears great until the first bump in the road. Lehman Brothers collapsed on 15th of September 2008 when reality hit the fan. It is an improved and systematized version of what I called "the politics of the lighting rod."

Lets unpack first how the disposable stories are built. We will contemplate what are their function later.

As said earlier a disposable story is built on facts that are mixed with half-truths, opinions and political spin. In our running example, the danish drones of September, you have: Facts: drones have been spotted around airfields. Half-truths: journalists arrested with drones near an airfield. Political spin: it is the Russians, they always wanted to destabilize us.

Let me save you some time: no one really knows what is going on, in itself, an essential characteristics of disposable stories: unlike scientific facts they are not falsifiable; they remain vague but are somehow linked to major trends, such as the war in Ukraine. A few internet searches would have you learn that there are on average 30 unauthorized drones a day that fly near Brussels airport. But who uses search engines in those AI times anyway? This little fact shows you that this disposable story could have been promoted almost any day. This is the second crucial feature of disposable stories: they superficially appear to be linked to current events but in fact they could be ignited almost at any time. What makes the confusion harder to spot is that they are used to support/weaken a current narrative. It is here that disposable stories reveal their true nature: they are essentially incentives in a distributed system designed to control the narrative.

The third important feature of disposable stories isn't really about the stories themselves. Stories become disposable because of our social media environment. Many stories should not have been disposable in the first place. I can think of hundreds of them: from the mystery around the Biden's administration —who was in charge?— to the real story behind the Nordstream sabotage passing by the firebombing of a Tesla truck in front of a Trump hotel in Las Vegas on January first and including two known assassination's attempts against the leading candidate during the last US presidential campaign. All those stories have the potential to change your worldview on their own. Yet no one seems to care enough to simply talk about them anymore. This is mainly because social media is a never-ending forward rush. The motto is not "Don't look up" but rather "Don't look back."

Program vs Protocol

The Watergate scandal is a polar opposite of a disposable story. It spanned across two years and was used to remove a president from his office. In many ways it operated like a computer program. The succession of news pieces was orchestrated by Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein, aided by the famous "Deep State" source, Deep Throat. The instructions were precise and came from the internal organs of the state apparatus.

Disposable stories, however, are more like a protocol. The difference with a program is that no one is compelled to follow a protocol. For instance, your web browser is not forced to adhere to the HTTP protocol. It is not like a C program that must follow a strict syntax otherwise it won't even compile nor produce an executable. Your web browser might not strictly follow HTTP for five minutes then to follow it; the worse that can happen is nothing. There is no external control over how your browser should behave: it is better if the browser follows HTTP protocol because you will have interesting results, but the implementation remains open and is left to the programmer's discretion.

Consider the drone story again: It generated a lot of exchanges on social media. However, unlike the Watergate press articles, these social media reactions are not controlled by an editorial board that follows a precise plan and timeline. The disposable story acts as loose guideline; it remains open to its environment. Your aunt who might have seen something can become part of the project by publishing her story on a Facebook post. She is not a source emanating from the "Deep State". Yet somehow she will contribute to the trend's growth. The story acts as a road sign, in the spirit of the lightning rod I mentioned earlier in this post, which was the first allegory that came to my mind when I noticed this phenomenon. You are never forced to follow a road sign.

On social media there is this added component: if you want to be seen you know that somehow you have to react to "the thing of the day" because of how the algorithm works. Nudging is the name of the game. At the beginning of social media it was used for commercial purposes —to sell you more stuff. Nowadays it has been refined and is used for political control.

The Ship of Theseus

Greek mythology offers to us stories that are another dual of disposable stories. The one around the ship of Theseus is worth remembering: The ship wherein Theseus and the youth of Athens returned from Crete had thirty oars, and was preserved by the Athenians down even to the time of Demetrius Phalereus, for they took away the old planks as they decayed, putting in new and strong timber in their places, insomuch that this ship became a standing example among the philosophers, for the logical question of things that grow; one side holding that the ship remained the same, and the other contending that it was not the same. — Plutarch, Life of Theseus 23.1

Like the ship, our media reality is changed one disposable story at a time. The outer structure of the world may look similar that of previous years: institutions remain the same, you are going to vote to fulfill your "civic duty" etc. But when the judge reads and applies the law, it will be done under a new paradigm. The judge's worldview has been modified disposable story after disposable story, just like a construction worker building a wall one brick at a time. The difference with disposable stories is that the whole building —a cathedral— is still standing but the material that has been used to build it has disappeared. Our mental landscape now resembles those laser mappings of the amazon that reveal ancient cities now covered with mold.
