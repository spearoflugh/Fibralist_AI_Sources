WYSIWYG was all the rage in the 90s. The novel idea was that you could see on the screen exactly what would later be printed on paper. You no longer had to use a programming language to produce a nicely formatted document. Fast-forward to today: AI has taken over the digital world. The gap between digital representation and reality has never been so wide. What you get—reality—has almost nothing to do with what you see on your screens. Cheat on the sheet It was first noticed in chess. This ancient game was the yardstick for AI at the dawn of the digital era. When computers became more powerful than any human on earth, circa 2000, the issue of cheating emerged. In this sense, chess can also be seen as a benchmark for how society adapts to the digital age. Numerous scandals, from the infamous “ Toiletgate ” to speculations over the death of Naroditsky (even if the official cause was later attributed to heart failure ), have since come to light in both amateur and professional chess. One direct consequence is the ever-increasing list of prohibited items in tournaments: smartphones must be switched off and kept away from your person—they must be handed over to the arbiter—and the playing area is strictly defined. On top of that, more and more tournaments are played with rapid time controls because they make cheating more difficult. One can transpose what happened in chess to many areas of “normal life” today, as generative AI has provided students and individuals with new ways to cheat. Of course, taking a picture of an exam and asking Claude to write the solution is the first thing that comes to mind. But HR managers performing remote interviews are likely chatting with a GPT. Likewise, a grade transcript could be modified with a prompt like “please change all my Fs to Bs and write glowing remarks about me” by a lazy student. It was possible before using various software but nowadays you don’t even have to be tech-savvy. Just asking nicely will do the trick. This trend does not stop with individuals. If you have tried to follow what is going on in the conflict between the USA/Israel and Iran, you have certainly noticed that most messages are fake AI videos or AI-doctored content. There is very little data coming directly from the no longer existing front lines . Every bit of information is first monitored and altered to suit a narrative. It might be those egregious videos from the White House or the head of the Iranian regime. It's AI slop all over the place. Taitsab The short pamphlet by F. Bastiat, That Which is Seen, and That Which is Not Seen (TWiSTWiNS) addresses a key economic principle known as the Broken Window Fallacy. What is seen is that breaking a window appears 'good' for the economy because it creates work for the repairman. What is not seen is that if the window had not been broken, the money spent on the repair could have been used to enrich society by producing something else—while still leaving the original window intact. If Bastiat were alive today he would certainly add a section on that which is generated by AI and that which is not generated by AI. He would find that the that which is not generated by AI is shrinking like the Magic Skin of Balzac . In the story a young man named Raphaël de Valentin acquires a supernatural piece of shagreen (la peau de chagrin) that grants his every wish but shrinks with each fulfilled desire. As the skin diminishes, so does Raphaël's physical vitality, creating a trade-off between the satisfaction of his appetites and the length of his life. The translation is roughly : IF YOU POSSESS ME, YOU SHALL POSSESS ALL. BUT YOUR LIFE WILL BELONG TO ME. GOD HAS WILLED IT SO. DESIRE, AND YOUR DESIRES SHALL BE FULFILLED. BUT MEASURE YOUR WISHES AGAINST YOUR LIFE. IT IS HERE. WITH EVERY WILL I SHALL SHRINK LIKE YOUR DAYS. DO YOU WANT ME? TAKE. GOD WILL GRANT YOUR PRAYER. — SO BE IT! Maybe you have heard this interview in which the CEO of openAI presents intelligence as a commodity ? It is hard not to see it as a digital version of La Peau de Chagrin . The AI feeds on reality to grant your wishes. Looking at social media, it seems that in some spaces, reality has already completely vanished. People used to say "touch grass" just a few weeks ago. Now, I am not sure they even have an idea of what grass is—beyond a statistical distribution of words that can be appropriately used in a textual context. Etymologically, 'context' is what surrounds a text. As we have just seen, context is disappearing before our very eyes. Perhaps you now better understand Trump's interventions regarding the war: he speaks of performing regime change, reaching a nuclear deal, or reopening a strait that was open before the conflict—and, by the way, the war is already won, and the allies are cowards for not helping us reopen that strait. If you approach these declarations as having a logical dependency, you have missed the spirit of the times. It means that you believe that the peau de chagrin is not going to disappear. Just like any soldier going to war is ignoring the risks and always imagine that others are going to die. TWiGAiTWiNGAi Another update has to be done. One on the idea that software is eating the world by Mark Andreesen. Today, the world is divided between what has been generated by AI and what has not. The world is not being eaten by software in general, but by a single software. AI is the One Ring that promises humans eternal power. In The Lord of the Rings , this represents the power of unaccountability; the Ring renders you invisible—just as using an AI to express yourself does.
