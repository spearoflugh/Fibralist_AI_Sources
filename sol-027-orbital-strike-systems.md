  <key_concepts>
    <concept name="Orbital Strike Platform — Technical Feasibility">
      A constellation of satellites each carrying standard anti-ship missiles (not nuclear, not exotic technology) can provide global strike capability. Key parameters: cost ≈ one modern aircraft carrier for global coverage; any point on Earth reachable in under 45 minutes; circular error precision 1-10 meters; ~30 satellites sufficient for permanent global coverage (vs 6,750 for Starlink). Already feasible with current off-the-shelf technology.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Advantages Over Carrier Strike Groups">
      No range issues (every target equidistant from orbit); faster to deploy (carrier groups take 2-3 weeks to reach theater); cost-efficient (750M$ carrier mission vs equivalent orbital capability); no thousands of personnel at risk; no fixed bases to defend or supply. The cost of one Arleigh Burke-class destroyer combat mission ≈ cost of launching equivalent orbital payload.
    </concept>
    <concept name="ATGM Trap — Scale Shifts Again">
      Anti-Tank Guided Missiles (1970s): "for a fraction of a tank's cost you can destroy it." Reality at scale: terrain, formations, mobility, artillery softening — the "ATGM solution" converges back to something that looks exactly like a tank. Orbital systems avoid this trap because they are inherently complex and multi-functional (sensors, communications, targeting, strike) unlike the single-purpose ATGM.
    </concept>
    <concept name="First Mover Incentive">
      Unlike nuclear weapons (mutual assured destruction creates stable deterrence), orbital strike systems are scalable — you can start with one satellite and scale gradually. No hard technological barriers like uranium enrichment. The first power to achieve orbital strike supremacy will be incentivized to prevent competitors from reaching equivalent capability. This creates an unstable race dynamic more dangerous than the nuclear balance.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Smartphone Can Launch Orbital Strike">
      The logical endpoint: smartphones can already communicate with Starlink satellites. At full development, orbital strike authorization could theoretically require only a smartphone. "Orbital strike systems are the military equivalent of social media: suddenly each human has been given the capacity to send a message to every human. Imagine the equivalent but for orbital strikes."
    </concept>
  </key_concepts>

I had again an interesting conversation with Grok AI. This was a few hours long autistic adjacent inquiry around space technology. More precisely low earth orbit (LEO) stuff. This was a direct follow up to my previous inquiries about how the lowering of the price of kg in LEO could change the whole geopolitical landscape. Interestingly I started the with this article in which the AI made a stupid error (confounding kilotons and tons) to investigate how kinetic strikes could become a thing following SpaceX results. It was followed by this one in which I was thinking about the implications on strategical terms. I also tackled the political and philosophical point of view in a third article here . So I took on this journey a little bit less than 6 months ago. I was struck at two levels by this new interaction with AI. The first one is how much AI has progressed. The thing is much more reliable and details its conclusions. I don’t see it as more intelligent, but rater less stupid than first versions. I see them as an extension of books. Rather as a way to access data than real intelligence. But I was struck in the same way that Kasparov was struck in the late 80s when he found out that he could used database to prepare his games: “In January 1987 I was back to play another ’simul’ against the Hamburg team. This time I had two days to prepare, so we dug out the names of all the players and checked their records in the computer. It was an eye-opener for me. It took about ten minutes to find 192 games. If I ask my trainers to find me a game, going through the books, it could take days. This time, armed with the information I needed, I beat six of the Hamburg team and drew with the other two. The result, 7-1, was extraordinary. They couldn’t believe it. Because I knew their habits, I could lead them into traps.” G. Kasparov in Chessbase is 25 , 2011. I had the exact same feeling. Granted, I am not Kasparov, but the quote encapsulates very well the experience. In this quote Kasparov is not relying on the machine to think for himself. It was useful to gather information —the games— then he was smart enough to know what to do about them —the traps—. In my conversation I was exploring a scenario of orbital strike platforms. Something like a satellite with a dozen of missiles (classical weapons). Say something like a standard anti ship missile fired from LEO. My aim was to test several scenarios to see whether they were realistic or not given our current level of technology . In the process I learned a lot about the different kind of orbits. I also learned a lot about the order of magnitude of several basic problems: how much it costs in fuel to re-enter the atmosphere, how much time it takes etc. I was blown out by the results : Grok was very precise (giving you the opportunity to check each step of the reasoning behind the answers) and also able to read between the lines (a bit of intelligence finally ?). I was really surprised by the quality of the answers. I have read some scientific papers and reports about the subject and I had this Kasparov’s feeling: I have learned by the scientific papers but it was much less easy than what I have learned during this session. This is the “If I ask my trainers to find me a game, going through the books, it could take days”. There was nothing really hard —basically Newtonian physics plus basic spatial geometry was enough— but if I had to do it by hand it would have taken me weeks. Again nothing very complex but you have to check things etc. The second level at which I was struck is the primary subject of this post. What I envisioned is actually much more doable than I was initially thinking. The Battle of Midway Syndrome The battle of Midway is of huge historical importance. It marks the end of the Japanese expansion and the entrance into a new era of warfare at sea. If blitzkrieg was the terrestrial version with combine arm tactics, the battle of Midway was the point from which the aircraft carriers began to dominate the seas. Many movies have been done about this battle, and for most western boys such movies are their only mental representation of what war between peers is. One reason is also that all the wars fought since (Korean war excepted and also Vietnam war to a less extent) where not against a peer enemy. Irak wars, Afghanistan and the most of the two decades following 9/11 have seen wars against terrorist groups. So it is quite natural to think that future major wars are going to look like a glorified version of Midway battle only changed with faster jets, more electronic etc. Nothing is further from the truth. In the midway battle sensors were rudimentary. Radars were seldom used and not used within aircraft. Likewise the pilot of an aircraft had to physically see , with his own eyes, the target. Missiles and drones where not invented yet, they came later in the war in the form of the Germans V1 and V2 . There is more than an coincidence with social media. Before social media you had to physically meet with other people. I don’t have to spend much to convince you that the dating world has changed because of the internet. The same is going to happen for war. Orbital strike platform might be one of this radical change Orbital Strike Platforms My session with Grok was about the technical, financial and technological issues related with an orbital strike platform. TL;DR it is already possible at all level, with our current level of technology. Nothing like secret laser stuff or even something approaching nuclear level stuff. Run of the mill spacial and missile technology offers solutions. The reduction of price to put mass in LEO already makes it feasible from a financial point of view. Remember that Starlink, a private entity, already manage a fleet of 6750 satellites. So nothing crazy like the space lasers or giant space Nazi mirror (yes it is a real thing called heliobeam or sun gun and was imagined in the 40s by mad Nazi scientists…). Maybe, surely, those kind of things will come, but they are not required. I am not going to delve into details but for a cost in the ballpark of one modern aircraft carrier, you can deploy a global strike system (detection and targeting cost included while they certainly already exist to some extent) with the following rough capabilities: Every point of earth can be hit in less than 45 min if a fleet of platforms is large enough (typically two or three dozen are enough to meet this requirement way less than Starlink). From decision to launch to impact with a circular error precision of 1 to 10 m. A single typical missile hit is largely enough to damage an aircraft carrier, rendering it impossible to use for fulfilling a mission, and around 0.5/1 kT depending on several factors. The cost of one combat mission for a typical US destroyer, say Arleigh Burke class , in a high intensity war is roughly equivalent to the cost of sending an equivalent amount of missiles in LEO (say 10 tomahawks) : around 100/200 million dollars. There are countless scenarios that I have ran through. I was shocked by how many were credible and in the ballpark of what is already done in terms of costs and availability. From a purely technical point of view orbital strike platforms offer many advantages over today’s strike platforms: No range issues . From orbit every target is at the same distance. If you choose a polar orbit then the rotation of the earth implies that a single platform will be able to target any point on earth (of course you may have to wait but having redundancy may offer you permanent capabilities just like Starlink gives you permanent internet connection). Faster to deploy . Carrier strike groups can project power but they require time. Typical time is 2 or 3 weeks to reach Middle-East form the US using the northern passage. On top of that many thousands personnel is impacted in the preparation of a strike group. Cost efficiency . Historically a mission of a carrier strike group in high intensity engagement, for instance during Libya or Syria strikes, costs around 750 million$, without the cost of ammunition used. With this amount of money, it is possible to put the mass enough for 250/500 strikes in orbit (depending on what kind of strike are scheduled). In terms of payload delivered by the orbital solution it amounts at something like twice more than the carrier strike group abilities (but each orbital strike is larger in impact, so you have this tradeoff of less flexibility/more power). Another point: given the rate of launches, it is already more than possible to make all the launches during the period of a standard carrier strike mission (30 days more or less). It is not important to delve further into details, but it is interesting though. The overall idea is that it is possible. It doesn’t require magic or secret technology (re-entry vehicles for MIRV are mastered for decades for instance). It is not that challenging and after this brief study I am even wondering whether or not it has already been partially implemented by major powers like the US, China or Russia. A major obstacle was the cost of such a solution. SpaceX costs have neutralized this barrier. But financial cost isn’t the only consideration. Zooming out Technical and financial considerations are just a small part of the global picture. This orbital strike proposition might recall the rising of Anti Tank Guided Missiles ( ATGMs ) at the end of the 70s. Suddenly the conversation about the fact that Tanks were no longer useful became all the rage. The one liner argument was something like this: “For the fraction of the cost of a Tank, you can destroy it with an ATGM, moreover it is a defensive weapon”. Reality was more complex. As it is often the case, problems occur as naturally as smile on the face of a happy baby when you switch scales. If the one-liner was ok to describe what happens on the testing field, the reality of high intensity war is another thing. Firstly at the individual level: the terrain where the fight happens is maybe more complex than a desert. Think forest in the middle of central Europe. It means that the theoretical range of the ATGM is largely a myth. In real life you will have few seconds to launch because you will just have a couple of hundred meters. Now zoom out a bit: you are not targeting a single tank. It is most probably a formation of dozen of tanks. It means that if you miss, they won’t miss you. Now zoom out more: tanks can move around. You can’t follow them with your feet. It means that you need a vehicle to move infantry. Now zoom out again: actually there is going to be an artillery softening before the tanks show up. Maybe it would be a good idea to have armored vehicles to resist shrapnel. Now you notice that tanks are not necessarily using roads, so your vehicle might be equipped with tracks. So to sum up you have an armored vehicle using tracks that are going to be used to download infantry so that they will be able to use their ATGM If you think again for 15 seconds you see that your ATGM solution really looks like… a Tank. Why bother download infantry and not put the ATGM on the vehicle ? And looking at realistic engagement circumstances you notice that a Gun is much more efficient. This little story is not to prove that ATGM are useless, they can be interesting in some scenarios like urban fighting, but that they were hardly the end of tanks either as many thought at first. Same story happened after Port Arthur disaster where Japanese torpedo boats destroyed much more costly Russian dreadnoughts (actually dreadnoughts continued to be the king of the seas until WWII and Midway battle from where aircraft carriers gained undisputed supremacy). Now the question is : why would it be different with orbital strike systems ? Why could they make tried and tested solutions obsolete ? Are they rather like ATGMs or like aircraft carriers in terms of military strategy ? A first hint that it is major breakthrough come from the fact that orbital systems are highly complex solutions. They are not directed to a simple goal like the ATGM were. With orbital systems you can do a lot of things. Firing missiles is just a little thing. Satellites are already used to deploy sensors, allow worldwide communications, provide geographic data (GPS) etc. It is not like a glorified bazooka. The fact that those systems are complex means that you can adjust them. It is difficult to adjust and ATGM, on the other hand a Tank is a big equipment on which you can add new tools (for instance Active Protection Systems ), you can also use the mobility of the tanks to adapt tactics etc. Same thing was true with dreadnoughts : you can put light projector, put anti torpedo nets around it when it is at anchor etc. A second hint is that orbital systems bring a new capacity to the table: the fact that each point of the earth is at the same distance is a major new capacity. No other weapon system have this capacity. Even ICBMs have a maximal range. There is no notion of range for a satellite with relation to earth targets. In that it is like the extension of the range of power projection provided by the airplanes with relation to the dreadnoughts limited gun range. Likewise martial questions are just the tip of the iceberg: It is not because something is technically feasible that it represent a tactical/strategic interest. Think at chemical warfare. It was first used during WWI but it showed limited operational interest (because you can’t move when you use it, you can’t control wind, it is actually harder to protect from an explosion etc.) . Moreover it has been seen as morally abhorrent (while war consists in killing and making severely injured people go figure), up to now at least, by major league combatants. Notice that Hitler never used chemical warfare on the battlefield. The reasons are complex and include the fear of retaliation. What is “allowed” during war is the emergent result of tacit agreement between fighters (and no it has barely anything to do with treaties, which at best are attempts at formalizing those tacit agreements). What can we say about that point of view with relation to orbital strike systems? First, from a technical point of view there are no limitations regarding treaties in place. The Outer Space Treaty bans weapons of mass destruction. In the scenarios that I was exploring it was just about standard missile, so technically not covered by the treaty. Another idea is that if such kind of strikes are at a sub-mass destruction level, they remain very powerful. Close to 1kT TNT equivalent. The orbital speed adds a sizeable kinetic energy to the payload of the missile (that can be useful for other purposes). Using current technology mostly large and almost fixed target can be envisioned. It is conceivable to strike an aircraft carrier or a large war ship. It is not yet, I mean by using on the shelf technology, possible to perform air superiority missions, but it is not impossible to imagine it down the road very quickly: you can slow down the missile and behave like a standard anti aircraft missile, but fired from space rather than from the ground. But other considerations will have to be weighted (the cost of the solution with relation to the price of the target for instance). You can’t escape much from the 20 min window required to go back to earth (it is possible to act on the margin by using more fuel but not a lot): an airplane can do a lot of things in a 20 min window, an aircraft carrier less so… The implication is that “useful” strikes will have very sizeable consequences. Is the destruction of an aircraft carrier (together with the thousand of casualties ensuing) going to mechanically escalate the conflict ? There is the same game theoretic issues than with the use of so called tactical nukes at a lower level. An orbital strike is not like an enhanced air strike. An orbital strike looks like something of a different nature. Another dimension is that orbital strikes are ridiculously easy to achieve once the system has been set up. Just a smartphone would be theoretically enough: smartphones can already communicate with satellites (starlink). Security issue aside, anyone with a smartphone in hand could be in capacity to launch an orbital strike. Orbital strike systems are the military equivalent of social media. Suddenly, with social media, each human has been given the capacity to send a message to every humans. It is not a manner of speech. It is real and happens everyday now. You can already see how TikTok videos have changed the world, now imagine the equivalent but for orbital strikes. If you think that just writing a treaty to forbid it is going to make it, I welcome you to take a job at the European Commission. The world will go on without you. There can be only one Our last remark is very much like the opening of Pandora’s box. Imagine how the world is going to be impacted, figuratively speaking this time, from such systems. Suppose that such a system is already deployed by Taiwan. Then the invasion of the island by continental China is no longer possible. Of course, China can still destroy Taiwan, but there is nothing new. But with a functional orbital strike systems, even an incomplete one, the invasion become impossible. Ships will be destroyed while crossing water and it is going to be even less possible to build a harbor (a fixed target) to unload troops and logistics. Likewise a blockade of the island becomes much more problematic and costly. On a more global scale, just like the nuclear bomb has changed geopolitics, orbital strike systems will alter how states behave between them. I am having hard times to think how such a world would emerge, even more what an equilibrium would look like. My initial thought is that the first one to achieve orbital supremacy will be incentivized to forbid any new actor from contesting this supremacy. This threat of the emergence of an hegemon looks more credible than what happened with the nuclear bomb. Indeed, orbital strike systems are more splitable than a thermonuclear explosion. Moreover, it is easier to scale orbital strike systems. There are no hard technological barriers like uranium enrichment etc. It is also gradual: it is possible to start from one missile and to go up to a pervasive system covering the globe 24/7. I am intuiting that the first power that is going to deploy such systems (the US?) will gain such an operational advantage that it is going to be used to forbid concurrents to reach similar level. It is also possible that current nuclear power won’t see that kindly and may interpret such threats as national security threats? It is anyone’s guess at this point. Most probably the real change will be impossible to forecast. For instance nuclear bombs have directly lead to the growing importance of special forces: as the war between peers became impossible because of the risk of global annihilation, then second hand wars and special operations became the focus. In other words being able to make large destruction directly induced small scale operations which is somewhat paradoxical. If one thing is certain, it is that unexpected consequences will soon appear because of the new capabilities offered by orbital strike systems.
