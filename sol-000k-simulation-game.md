    <concept name="Second Wave of Virtualization — Internalization">
      The first wave of virtualization was external: technologies making the virtual world
      more immersive (VR goggles, haptic suits, Meta). This wave failed — nobody cared about
      Meta. The second wave occurred *inside* us: once everyone has internalized the existence
      of the virtual sphere, we project our virtual mental models onto objective reality.
      "We are creating the world in the image of what is in our minds." The direction of
      causality has inverted: no longer does the world shape our mental models, we impose
      our (virtualized) mental models on the world. The COVID crisis is the empirical
      demonstration: a real but manageable threat was made to dominate all of reality
      for two years because the mental model was totalizing.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Colonel Chabert Syndrome">
      From Balzac's novella: a cavalry officer declared dead at war returns to find his
      entire life — wife, fortune, identity — legally and socially appropriated. Applied
      to the digital era: identity is no longer anchored in neighborhood (your neighbors
      knew who you were) but in cryptographic keys and digital accounts. You can be
      locked out of your digital life overnight — shadow-banned, account suspended,
      TOS changed — with no recourse, no due process, no community witness. The
      "Colonel Chabert" is anyone whose digital identity has been severed from their
      physical existence by a platform's version-controlled rules (sol-031 Moral Version
      Control). The Balzac theme also points to competing worldviews: the Napoleonic
      world of honor vs. the money-only world. Today the digital worldview is replacing
      the analog worldview with the same violence.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Mental Jail — The Limitative Power of Imposed Models">
      The creative power of mental models (Michelangelo finding David in the marble —
      choosing a viewpoint "builds" reality) has a dual: the limitative power. An obsessive
      mental model starts to be seen everywhere, bending all of reality around itself.
      This is the structural mechanism behind Homeopathogenesis (sol-013) and Disposable
      Stories (sol-036): once a narrative frame is imposed, all events are read through it,
      and the frame becomes self-reinforcing. The digital world accelerates this because
      the algorithms reward model-consistent content and filter out model-disconfirming content.
    </concept>

There is this famous scene in "When Harry met Sally" in which Meg Ryan, in the middle of a crowded diner, shows to Billy Crystal that she can simulate an orgasm very convincingly. Few days ago an international photo prize has been awarded to a picture that was later admitted as having been produced by AI. It looks like (pun semi intended) that wherever we turn our eyes, simulations or artificial productions are on sight.

## It is all in our head now

We are hitting the second wave of virtualization. The first wave was the invention of technologies and tools making the virtual world more real. If it sounds paradoxical it is because it is. It was like a race to get more and more realistic video games, immersive experiences etc. This race culminated with Meta. You remember Meta right? It seems no one gives a shit about it a year or so after one of the biggest players of the internet decided to go all in about virtual reality. The funerals took place in private and the layoffs were massive. What Zuckerberg didn't see coming was the second wave of virtualization.

I don't know why we all thought that virtualization was always going to be an external thing. The idea being that you were going to have more and more immersive experiences but through technological artefacts: goggles, gloves and suits with haptic feedback, you name it. The second wave occurred in ourselves. Now that *everyone* has internalized the existence of the virtual sphere, we are actually building the world that matches our views of the world. We are now projecting onto the objective world our perceptions of the *virtual reality*. In the same way that God created us in His image, we are creating the world in the image of what is in our minds.

## Mental jail

In his interview in the Lex Fridman podcast, Edward Frenkel has this remark: is it possible that the observer creates his own reality? This question can be discussed from many angles. The idea is that by deliberately choosing a viewpoint you "build" reality. This is the old idea that Michelangelo simply found David in the marble. The statue was there, in the rock, just waiting to be exposed. This is the creative aspect of our ability to shape the world at our whim. Another aspect of the same ability is the dual of creative: it is also very limitative. You can become so obsessed with a single idea that suddenly you start to see it everywhere. Before you know it, the world starts to spin around it. Have I to recall you how much the COVID crisis was an illustration of that? For the best part of 2 years everything in the world turned around a sickness that is serious but nowhere near close to historic killers like the plague or cholera. When one looks at the numbers without passion the most charitable conclusion is that the threat was grossly overstated. Nevertheless it lasted months after the truth was known without any doubt left. This real life experiment shows that one should not take too lightly this propensity to limit ourselves by, sometimes wrong, our own mental models.

## The Colonel Chabert Syndrome

"Colonel Chabert" is a part of Balzac's "La Comédie Humaine" series. The story, in a few words, goes like this: Chabert is a cavalry officer serving under Napoleon. He is severely wounded during a battle and is saved by local peasants. As the recovery takes years people in Paris think that he is dead. When he comes back all his life has been robbed — his wife remarried, his fortune has been spoilt etc.

There are so many points of interest to consider this story under the light of our modern days. Maybe the fact of being locked out of his life is nowadays a technical possibility comes first. It was not really possible before because proofs of identity were not in numbers (cryptographic keys) but in our neighborhood — in the literal sense: your neighbors were the ones who could identify you. Now that large parts of our lives are virtual they are more susceptible to being hacked or inaccessible. You can be a victim of identity theft, but you can also be on the wrong side of a sudden political shift of your preferred social media (or they may change the TOS every 3 weeks without giving a shit about whether you agree or not). It goes both ways. Moderation is fluid.

Another point of interest of "Colonel Chabert" is the theme of competing worldviews. Chabert is an image of the Napoleonic times in which honor and military virtue represented everything. This worldview has supposedly died on the battlefield. It is replaced with a world in which the only thing that matters is money. The world is changing so fast that, to be fit with our times, we have to become strangers to ourselves.

I don't know how my 1990 me would feel today (and I was on the internet since 1992). The shift was gradual but the pace never stopped from accelerating. The virtual world has changed more than the objective world. It's like when you visit a city where you lived a couple of decades later. Buildings are more or less the same, people lost hair, got older, put on some weight... The future has always been a mystery; it turns out that the present is also a mystery to us now.
