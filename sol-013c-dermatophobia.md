I grew up as son of two medical doctors. My father was working in emergency (SAMU), my mother was more interested in dermatology. They had loads of medical books at home. I spent a non-negligible time of my childhood reading and looking at those books. You think you know what disgusting means because you saw a couple of gore TikTok videos? Think again. There is nothing more disgusting than dermatology books. So maybe I was desensitized from an early age.

When you present medical literature to normal people they generally start to see the symptoms everywhere. Surely it is a phase. This idea is well captured by Chuck Palahniuk's Victor Mancini character in "Choke". Victor is a medical student dropout, and there is this phase where he sees everywhere every disease that he has just learned about at college. It is a perfect encapsulation of a societal trend of our digitized times. People are saturated by a new problem. The problem invades the media sphere, then enters their mind and from there develops completely out of touch with reality.

## To Fantasy Land and Beyond

With internet all the knowledge of mankind is just a click away from you. But as your time is limited you have to choose. More and more even choosing has begun to be too costly and the choice is delegated to an AI. But when you focus on a subject you can fall into endless rabbit holes. For any subject you can find theories, real life experiences, fun or gore videos. There is probably more material than you can ingest through your whole life. As the AI spots your preferences, even when you are not actively looking for the subject in particular, you might find your TL flooded by the subject. The results of your internet searches will be influenced by this, etc. Self-fulfilling prophecy comes to mind.

Lets consider the last trendy subject: bed bugs in France. For a few weeks now there is a huge media coverage of a supposedly invasion of bed bugs in Paris. Just like in dermatology books what you see in reports are the extreme cases. Picture a poor neighborhood with an old lady completely overwhelmed in her condo in which sanitary conditions are dire. Add few pictures of body parts covered with bed bug bites and you are ready to unfold the machine: climate change, Airbnb, nature rebels vs human, capitalism (because increase of travels)… you name it.

The fact that it is this time of the year — people come back from holidays, kids go back to school and return home with lice — explains in part this new trend. Here is a piece of anecdotal evidence. I know a person who became completely phobic with relation to bed bugs. She changes clothes when entering an apartment, is afraid of using public transportation, half of her conversations are about that, and I could only begin to imagine how much she has spent on TikTok looking at short videos about the subject. This is your typical gen Z late adolescent/young adult. She has very few contact with nature (while talking about it a lot), many abstract knowledge (or second hand). Clearly she is obsessed by the subject and you can literally see her imagination run wild live. As a medical student she sees what she has just learned everywhere. If you listen to her it is nothing short of a biblical plague. During a conversation she told me that a single bug can have as much as 300 eggs or so. So she was discovering exponential growth out of her fears rather than math class. When I simply asked her how come the world is not covered by bed bugs there was a glitch. Few seconds during which she was like in the air, no longer supported by her theory that *we are all going to be infected*… But it didn't last. Cognitive dissonance is not an easy place to stay.

The fact that it happens in her mind and mostly her mind makes it hard to discuss. Because every common sense argument is buried under tons of "facts" coming from the internet. The distinctive feature is the hyper focus on a single issue. Her view of the world revolves around that single problem. Just like during COVID. At this point I think that Mattias Desmet's theory is the most appropriate to describe the situation: a free-floating anxiety finds its way to incarnate into something tangible. Social media helps both in generating this kind of anxiety — because it produces people detached from objective reality — and propagating it — as all your friends talk about it, then it is proof that it is important even if you can't see it directly in your reality.

It is already concerning at an individual level. The problem is, if history teaches us anything, that it scales very horribly.

## Germs and Authoritarianism

The most potent social feeling is disgust. Sex is maybe more powerful but it doesn't scale. Fear can be scaled and used to manipulate crowds quite effectively. Disgust is as powerful as fear, scales just as well, but the social response to fear is way less destructive than the response to disgust. You avoid what scares you. You destroy what disgusts you. The fact that authoritarianism is tightly correlated with transmissible disease levels and parasite prevalence should not surprise.

I suspect that disgust is also better adapted to a digitized society than fear. Of course with a "good" disease you can have both. But if I had to bet I would put my money on disgust on the long run. Because at the end of the day it is difficult to be scared behind a screen. Virtuality renders threats more distant. This is by the way linked to those ideas of microaggression. So my prediction is that social policies will be more and more strict. If you want to have an image of the future imagine Karen with an aerosol of insecticide in hand.
