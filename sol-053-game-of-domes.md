By many measures, our news space is starting to look like a version of Game of Domes. You have the visible Iron Dome: missiles and anti-missiles are those modern-day killing fireworks for which we have a new episode every day, with plot twists involving evading missiles and questions regarding the supply chain for interceptors. Then there is the Golden Dome, the one no one sees but which occupies POTUS's talks and dreams. There is also a semi-visible Dome — the Silicon Dome — which is the subject of this piece.

## Nylon Dome

In the late 1920s, the chemical giant DuPont decided to invest in "pure" science. After years of experimenting with polymers, Carothers and his team created a fiber that was strong, elastic, and heat-resistant: Nylon. The name was chosen by DuPont through an incremental process, starting from hundreds of suggestions by a committee. They began with "No-Run" because the stockings wouldn't "run" (develop holes). But "No-Run" was a false claim — the stockings could still run — so they moved to "Nuron." However, that sounded too much like "neuron." It was changed to "Nilo." "Nilo" was flipped to "Olin," then changed back. They eventually landed on "Nylon."

I always thought it stood for "Now You Lose Old Nippons." Somehow, that seemed plausible to me. This story popped into my mind because of the recent invasion of my social media timeline by Japanese X (formerly Twitter). It turns out I am not alone; this is a direct decision by the company. Beyond personal experience, there is this statement from the owner:

> Elon Musk: "Japan X posts excellent content that is well-deserving of global exposure."

Why the algorithm has changed globally is anyone's guess at this point. I could make a case based on geopolitics: the USA are pivoting to Asia, NATO is on its last legs, and Trump has already suggested that US troops might be removed from Germany. This could be a way to prime public opinion for coming battles in Southeast Asia. Or, perhaps it is simply because Tesla is going to invest massively in Japan and Elon wants to stay on good terms with future consumers. A blend of both, or something else entirely, is possible.

I have no realistic way of knowing other than to wait and remember — which is one of the reasons I write — that this occurred almost overnight. This point is clear and interesting in itself.

## Pylon Dome

In real life, you need pylons to sustain a dome. The modification of timelines to grant more exposure to "Nippon Twitter" is the equivalent in the conversational space. The dome is a giant architectural project designed to protect you from small incoming projectiles. For your safety.

What hasn't been done for "your safety" since 9/11? Much like the Iran war — another gigantic undertaking in the name of safety — which was initiated as a "pre-emptive strike" in retaliation for another pre-emptive strike, according to M. Rubio. A kind of pre-pre-emptive thing.

Maybe the Nipponification of timelines is just a pylon and not the dome itself. Again, it's hard to know. It is ironic that in this age of networks and instant knowledge, the "weather" on the global public square is actually set by forces beyond the reach of the layman. We talk about democracy all day long, yet the powers that be are no longer in the old institutions. Deep tech is taking over the deep state.

"Pylon" also appears as a founder in Greek mythology. If you needed further proof of the timelessness of these myths, here it is: Pylon was the King of Megara and is credited with founding the city-state of Pylos. In mythology, founding a city was seen as an act of divine favor. Any resemblance to Elon is, of course, purely coincidental.

## Tyrone Dome

The name Tyrone comes from County Tyrone in Northern Ireland — specifically from the Irish *Tír Eoghain*, meaning "Land of Owen." If I am to trust the now-defunct 23andMe, it turns out I hail from there as well.

When you look closely at a nylon stocking, you see a mesh of fibers. Things are intertwined. It looks like a mess at that level, but if you zoom out, the global shape begins to emerge. Our lives on social media are like those fibers. You have a local understanding of what is going on.

The Iron Dome is made of missiles you can see. The Golden Dome exists in expressed dreams, but the Silicon Dome remains invisible and untouched by online conversations. One of Mao Zedong's famous quotes is: "The guerrilla must move amongst the people as a fish swims in the sea." Today's revolutionaries are no longer peasants or blue-collar workers. This is another inversion of the digital age: revolutions now start within the elite class. A digital account is merely a pawn in a civilizational shift. "Free speech" is corralled by forces that are felt rather than seen. Have you posted about Japan lately?
