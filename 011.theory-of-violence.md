<title>A Fibralist Theory of Violence</title>

This is piece is a preliminary reflection on the raw foundation of political organization in a digital society. A Fibralist theory of violence. If one accepts Carl Schmitt’s friend/enemy distinction as what politics is about, then a theory of violence is the how. From a purely abstract point of view, violence is the translation of a will, an idea, into physical contingencies.

It departs from civil engineering in that, in the case of violence, one is opposing another will. Building a bridge is the transformation of the physical environment; consider cybersecurity instead, where your opponent is a living entity, not an earthquake, wind, or other weather conditions. The difference is that in cybersecurity, your opponent is truly after you. An immobile defense is not going to make it. The emotional state when battling a machine that won’t start is not, and should not be, the same as when encountering an opponent in the ring. Yet, superficially, in both cases it appears that the world is against you.

The question is: from a Fibralist point of view what is institutional violence? What shape shall it take? This is the question of how the switch to a digital culture will affect the expression of the friend/enemy distinction.

Lets first examine how violence evolved during the last cultural shifts.
A History of Institutionalized Violence

It is hard to know what were, if any, the theory of violence before writing. The fact that polytheism appeared widespread suggests amongst other things that the unconscious was not internalized. There is also this interesting remark that God is vanishing in the Bible as noted by Julian James in The Origin of Consciousness and the breakdown of the Bcameral Mind. In both cases it shows that inner impulses were seen as external beings imposing their will on humans. There is this explicit story of Elijah in the book of Kings (Kings 1 chapter 19):

    12 After the earthquake came a fire, but the Lord was not in the fire. And after the fire came a gentle whisper. 13 When Elijah heard it, he pulled his cloak over his face and went out and stood at the mouth of the cave.

This process of internalization is also the one of a kind of objectification of violence. As the written culture spread, codes, laws and bureaucracy too. The savage battles in the field are replaced by trials and court rooms. Maybe something like bureaucracy taking over chivalry.

From a technical point of view now in oral cultures, violence is often somatic and immediate. It is tied to physical presence and honor.

    Oral Culture: Violence is a face-to-face “ritual” (the duel, the vendetta). It is limited by local memory and the reach of the human voice.

    Written Culture: Writing enables distance. One can order an execution or a war with a mere mark on parchment. Violence becomes “proactive” and administrative. The executioner no longer needs to be angry; he simply follows a code.

In the same way that consciousness is internalized, violence is institutionalized. Violence is no longer a response between individuals, but rather the Institution’s response to an Infraction. Ultimately, an Abstraction of the Enemy occurs: in oral culture, the enemy is the person who has wronged you; in written culture, the enemy becomes a legal category (the “outlaw,” the “heretic,” the “enemy of the State”), defined by texts without you ever having met them.

The vocabulary itself tells a clear story: “justice” derives from Ius. In Latin, Ius is a complex term merging three distinct ideas:

    The Ritual Formula: Originally, Ius was not a written text but a precise oral formula that had to be recited for an action to be legally valid.

    The Body of Rules: It represents the collective rules that govern life in society.

    The Physical Site: By extension, Ius also refers to the specific place where the magistrate administers justice.

Lets now address how violence is transformed in a digital era. This analysis is important into forecasting the challenges that have to be tackled under Fibralism.

Digital Violence

Digital culture is a strange hybrid of oral and written civilizations, infused with entirely new features. Regarding the evolution of violence, the most critical points are:

    The Ladder of Abstraction. This represents a move up the abstraction ladder: information no longer has direct material constraints. In a written society, you still have “paperwork”—the physical reality of paper. In a digital society, access to knowledge is tantamount to network access. One direct implication is the loss of the physical dimensions of information. On one hand, we have access to data regardless of its origin (a topological revolution); on the other, we have the ephemeral nature of “the cloud.” Data is present without being truly “here.” You cannot lay your hands on Terms of Service that evolve in real time—a stark contrast to the immutable stone tablets of the Code of Hammurabi.

    The Return to Immediacy. Most interactions are now performed live, a consequence of the ubiquity of networks. Regarding violence, this brings us closer to the pre-literate era. The “mechanical slowness” of writing has been mitigated, meaning people have less time for reflection. We have returned to the outburst of rage. However, this violence is not as physical as it once was; instead, it is expressed through social media swarms. This digital mob mentality is rendered possible by technology: no matter where you are, you are informed of the latest trend instantly. Being able to react without filters creates the volatile social media environment we see today.

    Precision and Coordination in the Objective World. Digital technologies also have profound implications for the physical world, specifically regarding precision and coordination. These two aspects allow for the transformation of consumer-grade products into military-grade assets. It is one thing to possess a stick of dynamite; it is quite another to strap it to a drone with sub-meter GPS precision or image-processing software capable of tracking specific targets. When digital communications are used to synchronize such attacks, it introduces a level of lethality previously reachable only by large-scale national armies.

From this analysis, the new challenge of the digital era appears clearly. Violence has become simultaneously more abstract and more immediate — more precise in its execution, more impulsive in its triggering. The state’s monopoly on institutionalized violence based on bureaucratic organization is structurally incompatible with a digital environment where a consumer drone can deliver military-grade lethality and a social media swarm can destroy a reputation in hours.

The first shield is culture. As violence becomes more distributed, centralized control becomes less effective — not because it lacks will, but because it lacks bandwidth and has a bad reactivity. No bureaucracy can process the speed at which digital violence operates. What can absorb it is a cohesive society — one where the norms are internalized deeply enough that they function without institutional enforcement. This is precisely what a strong and homogeneous culture provides: not a set of rules imposed from above, but a set of reflexes shared from within.

This points toward a fundamental principle of distributed system design that applies equally to political architecture: positive incentives are more robust than negative ones. In a centralized system, compliance can be enforced through punishment — the Prussian model, the Leviathan. In a distributed system, punishment is logistically impossible at scale. Also notice that the evanescent nature of internet swarms make retaliations more difficult against collective actions. What works instead is a protocol architecture where participants gain nothing by defecting and lose everything by being excluded. Bitcoin does not prevent double-spending by threatening miners — it makes cheating structurally unrewarding. A Fibralist defense culture operates on the same logic: the Fibrizen does not refrain from defection because he fears punishment, but because his entire identity — social, legal, cultural — is bound to the Fiber. Exclusion is the ultimate sanction precisely because belonging is the ultimate reward.

Historically, ancient Rome developed a specific body of law, administered by the office of the Praetor Peregrinus, to deal with such cases. When a Praetor took office, he didn’t just “wing it”; he issued an Edictum Perpetuum (a “Permanent Edict”). This was a public document posted in the Forum, listing all the legal formulas and remedies he pledged to grant during his term. The social tools used to restrain the power of these judges were:

    Public Accountability: Once the Edict was posted, the Praetor was legally bound by it. He could not change the rules mid-case to favor a friend without facing massive public and political backlash.

    The Lex Cornelia known as Praetor’s Edict : This law explicitly forbade Praetors from departing from their own edicts.

In the digital era, these mechanisms must be reimagined. The goals of public accountability and the permanence of the edict are now sought through blockchains. In modern terms, this is called on-chain transparency. This concept is pervasive in digital societies: because digital technologies are so nimble, they require robust mechanisms to ensure stability.

A trusted track record for assessing reputation could appear at every level of Fibrization. An equivalent to the Lex Cornelia would ensure that if a judgment is an internal Fiber affair, the rules cannot be altered arbitrarily, much like “Terms of Service” that are locked.

There is also the case of violence between two non-Fibrizens—the ‘guest’ status discussed in the Manifesto—within an area controlled by a Fiber. The LSP implies that the Fiber must ensure civil peace within its borders; consequently, such instances of violence cannot be ignored. The primary response should be expulsion. Furthermore, only Fibrizen institutions shall determine whether an incident warrants such a measure, as non-Fibrizens have little standing in the matter. The process is administrative rather than judicial in nature, at least from the non-Fibrizen point of view. The idea that the crux of the conflict is not the Fiber business.
Digital Proportionality

The digital mob belongs to the same category as the last one mentioned in the previous section. It is a form of oral violence — immediate, tribal, operating without due process — but amplified to industrial scale and made permanent by its digital footprint. Crucially, a social media swarm is by definition composed mostly of non-Fibrizens, or at least of individuals whose Fibrizen status cannot be verified. It is therefore, structurally, an instance of external violence — and should be treated as such rather than as an internal judicial matter.

What makes digital mob violence distinct from any prior form is a structural breach of proportionality: the duration of the sanction bears no relationship to the gravity of the offense. A local and ephemeral error becomes global and permanent. A remark made in passing leaves a searchable trace for decades. Neither oral law nor written law ever had to address this asymmetry — in oral culture, memory fades; in written culture, archives are inaccessible to most. Digital culture combines the worst of both: the immediacy of oral rage and the permanence of written record.

A Fibralist response to this requires two distinct mechanisms. The first is a doctrine of digital proportionality — an explicit norm, enforced within the Fiber, that sanctions must be calibrated to offenses and that permanent reputational damage cannot result from minor infractions. The second is a question of standing: when the target of a digital mob is a Fibrizen, the swarm constitutes an attack on the Fiber itself, not merely on an individual. This transforms the response from a private matter into a collective one — the Fiber’s equivalent of the Praetor’s Edict applied outward rather than inward.
Organized Violence in a Digital environment

Our previous observation naturally leads to the question of organized violence. Police and military operations differ not merely in scale, but in their very essence. Police actions are governed by the rule of law, whereas military action is an emergent property of the conflict itself.

To put it bluntly: Hitler refrained from using chemical gas on the battlefield not out of respect for abstract law or moral hesitation—as the existence of gas chambers grimly proves—but out of fear of retaliation. The 2026 war in Iran further illustrates this principle: there exists a “language of violence” where reprisals are precisely calibrated to specific attacks (for instance, Iran targeting Qatar’s natural gas fields only after Israeli forces bombed South Pars).

The Fibralist Manifesto is explicit: it is not a theory of military expansionism. Instead, its concept of defense is rooted in uncompromising deterrence. A fundamental tenet is that a “Fiber” serves, primarily, as a repository of memory. Consequently, retaliation against any state or organization that attacks a Fiber for what it represents must be dictated by the history of past confrontations.

Consider this progression: if an adversary inflicts X damage, the Fiber responds with 2X. The next encounter triggers 4X, then 8X, and so on. This is exponential retaliation. While the counter-strike may not be immediate due to strategic context, the adversary must exist in the absolute certainty that it is coming. From a Fibralist perspective, settling the score is seen as a primary raison d’être of the entire enterprise.

Much remains to be considered; these were merely preliminary observations. Among future topics, the question of how a state of war might be declared within a distributed Fiber is paramount. This leads directly to the broader issue of exceptionalism within a “Fibrization.”

This is a profoundly delicate subject: while a society cannot function without exceptional arrangements, recent history has demonstrated the need for extraordinary precautions. Without them, the exception inevitably bleeds into public life, threatening to subvert institutions and turn the established order upside-down.
