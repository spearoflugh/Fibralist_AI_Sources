      A Möbius strip looks like it has two sides, but has only one — you can traverse
      its entire surface without ever switching sides. Applied to electoral politics:
      every cycle produces a demagogue promising radical change (Obama → Guantanamo
      never closed, drone strikes escalated; Trump → Russia Gate, swamp wins; Biden →
      "unity," record division). The "twist" that created the Möbius strip in politics
      is the public-private partnership between states and global internet corporations:
      states need corporations to operate in the digital world; corporations need states
      for physical force. The resulting structure has no "other side" — the impression
      of alternation is topological artifact, not real choice. Elections become
      "increasingly irrelevant" not by conspiracy but by distributed system architecture.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Byzantine Fault Tolerance of the Globalist System">
      Formal application of Byzantine fault tolerance to political systems: a distributed
      system can function correctly with up to 1/3 compromised nodes. Losing one election
      (one node) does not collapse the system. The Milei or Trump experiments are single
      compromised nodes in a Byzantine-fault-tolerant network — the system absorbs them.
      This is "the antithesis of the butterfly effect": one unexpected actor does not
      cascade. The implication: only a coordinated failure of more than 1/3 of nodes
      (institutions, corporations, states simultaneously) could produce systemic change.
      All electoral strategies that focus on winning single elections misunderstand the
      architecture of the system they are trying to change.
    </concept>

A Möbius strip is this surprising topological object that looks like a ribbon but has only one side. It has strange properties like being a non-orientable surface. It means that when moving along the strip you are not able to determine whether you are moving clockwise or counter clockwise. Moving without having any way to know you are advancing or going backwards may ring a bell, isn't it? What is interesting is how you build a Möbius strip. It is relatively easy: a piece of paper and adhesive tape are enough. You just have to twist the strip of paper and attach it to itself. The allegory of our media environment couldn't be more on point.

## Towards a dualistic monism

When looking at a ribbon you can see that there are two faces. A Möbius strip also looks like that. But if you progress without interruption on the surface of a Möbius strip you will explore every inch of the strip without having to switch face. There is no need to jump from one face to the other one. It is done smoothly, almost imperceptibly.

Every resemblance with recent election cycles is not by accident. The infinite cycle looks like this: People are not happy with how things work. As a result a modern demagogue runs the election and pretends he will break everything down. Try Obama: the young Afro-American star that was going to shut down Guantanamo. He never did and droned to death more than any previous presidents combined. Trump is the same: he promised to end the swamp only to get stuck in the mud of Russia Gate and not be reelected. In short the swamp ended him. Biden promised unity. Divisions in the political area have never been this marked. Every time that you promise to change everything, things continue to evolve seemingly on their own. The description given by Putin — you can take it with the appropriate grain of salt — does not appear crazy off the bat:

> "Presidents come and go, but the policy does not change. Do you know why? Because the power of the bureaucracy is very strong. The person gets elected, he comes with some ideas, and then people come to him with briefcases, well dressed and in dark suits, and they begin to explain what he should do — and everything changes at once."

The internet social media environment is strange because there are not really "behind the scene" anymore. Everything appears as being visible — there is only one side of the Möbius Strip. There is no other side of the story anymore. Yet it looks like there are invisible forces at play.

## Let's twist again

Just a little twist is required to build a Möbius strip. What was the twist? The increasingly more intricate public-private partnership and the rise of global internet corporations might be it. They serve one another: there are no borders for internet-based corporations — they can impose their standards everywhere in a matter of seconds (think banning an app from the Apple or Google store). On the other hand global corporations have no way to implement force — that remains a prerogative of states. States, because of constitutions and legislation, can't act easily in the digital world. We have a win-win partnership. It was the social media platforms that banned Trump (it is not censorship if done by private companies, right?) not the state apparatus.

In such an environment elections appear more and more irrelevant. The hard point is that it is not clear where power really resides today: in historical institutions? In corporations? In their relationships? It bears more than superficial resemblance with distributed systems. In distributed systems it is hard to pinpoint where the system is — is it on the client side or server side? It exists in the dance between the two. Disconnecting one node is not going to make the whole system collapse. Said in another way: from the globalist side, losing one election is at worst like a node that is compromised in a distributed system. The system continues to work as if nothing happened — see how Bitcoin quickly recovered from being banned by China. This is the Byzantine fault problem: up to 1/3 of failing nodes, the system can continue to work properly. I don't expect that the Milei experiment will bring a lot for this reason. At best he is one failing node. It is the antithesis of the butterfly effect. It is not going to be enough to twist the ribbon another time.

## Separation of powers

This idea that we are spending time looking for "the other side" of a Möbius strip made me realize that what were called "powers" are no longer what they used to be. The theory formalized by Montesquieu relied on the identification of three powers: legislative, executive and judiciary. Our increasingly digital society is producing new kinds of powers and makes the older ones obsolete. In my opinion this is why the political stage is at best useless nowadays: it is about something that is no longer existing. More on this later.
