  <key_concepts>
    <concept name="Hyperpresent">
      The unstoppable convergence tendency of digital societies. Hyperpresent is to digital societies what gravity is to celestial objects — an attracting force that pulls all content, behavior, appearance, and discourse toward a single synchronous moment. Produced by two digital mechanisms acting together: (1) the capacity to synchronize without hierarchical structure (social media swarms), and (2) digital convergence — in the virtual world, everything is ultimately 0s and 1s, making all content formally equivalent and mutually comparable. Stars (celebrities) were the first to react to this meta-trend.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Omnicause">
      The convergence of unrelated political causes under a single social media trend. Term coined by Alysia Ames. Example: "Free Palestine + Fight Climate Change" signs at the same demonstration (November 2023). Mechanism: (1) social media acts as gravitational force — to be heard, you must leverage trending topics; (2) your timeline shows only what is trending, regardless of your personal concerns. The Omnicause is not irrationality — it is the rational response to an incentive architecture that rewards attaching to whatever is currently amplified.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Omniface">
      The physical convergence of aesthetic appearance among high-status individuals, driven by: non-aging social media profiles (your photo doesn't age even if you do), panurgization of cosmetic surgery and GLP-1 drugs driven by celebrities observing each other through social media. A picture-is-worth-a-thousand-words observation: all very rich women in 2025 are starting to look like the same alien. Digital convergence "fleshed out" — the virtual uniformity becoming physical uniformity.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Omnicandidate">
      The flattening of job candidate differentiation through AI-mediated interviews. Remote interviews hijacked by AI produce a uniformization of self-presentation analogous to online chess cheating (a problem running 15+ years). The same dynamic as Omniface but in professional rather than physical appearance. The algorithm both enables and enforces convergence.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Kafka's The Trial as Digital Allegory">
      K is arrested and put on trial for undisclosed reasons — the system is opaque, its logic inaccessible. Digital social media works identically: what is published will be read and used against you by unknown people for unknown reasons. The uniformity is simultaneously accepted (no one forces you to use AI or cosmetic surgery) and imposed (to remain relevant, you cannot do otherwise than follow the crowd). And unlike before: you cannot pretend not to know what the crowd is doing.
    </concept>

<content>
Maybe you've already heard about the concept of the Omnicause. The term was coined by Alysia Ames. The idea behind this concept could fill a few hours of a serious podcast, but I think a single picture from November 2023 (remember, the war that followed October 7, 2023, hadn't even started at that time) is better for grasping the concept.

[Image: protest signs combining "Free Palestine" and climate change slogans]

Let the image sink in, as they say. Of course, it appears completely stupid at face value. Even if you think that freeing Palestine and fighting climate change are moral imperatives, joining both ideas should appear, at the very least, strange if not suspicious. There have been many articles written about the Omnicause and how this concept helps us understand new forms of dissent. Here, I'm tackling the concept from a technological angle and exploring its implications. First, I will explore, through various examples, what the 'Omni' phenomenon owes to technological contingencies. Second, I will develop the concept of hyperpresent, which encapsulates the concrete impacts on our digital societies.

Omnicause

Citizens of the 21st century see and are seen through the lenses of social media. Their timelines are how they interact with the global discussion on the networks. This state of affairs acts as a very efficient incentive architecture to push for the flourishing of the Omnicause. Indeed, it works both ways: If you want to grab attention, you must leverage trending topics. Hence, social media acts as a gravitational force. Everyone who is craving to be heard will be attracted by the thing of the day. By construction, what you see on social media is what is trending. This means that whatever your personal mood and thoughts, you are going to be algorithmically dragged to the subject du jour.

These are purely virtual processes. One could think that they should remain locked in the realm of ideas. But that's not the case, because social media are used to synchronize people across the world. That is why the death of George Floyd in Minneapolis can lead to very large demonstrations in Europe in a few days (under lockdown because of the COVID-19 pandemic). In this case, too, the initial protest against police brutality started to aggregate all sorts of other things, from environmentalists to feminists passing by anti-capitalists.

This 'Omni' convergence extends beyond ideas to physical appearances, as seen in what I call the Omniface.

Omniface

Aging in the digital age is not the same as what aging was. One aspect is that your social media profile doesn't age. Maybe some flags are switched, and profile pictures too. What the odds are that those pictures haven't been filtered or AI-enhanced is anyone's guess, but they are not zero. On top of that, celebrities are looking at one another through social media, and it visibly leads to a massive panurgization — a phenomenon that I had forecasted a few years back on theoretical grounds. Cosmetic surgery and pills are subject to fashion, too.

Omnicandidate

Digital technologies do not limit themselves to social media. AI is taking its part in the same process. Here it leads to the uniformization of discourse. More and more job interviews, remote ones, are being hijacked by AI. The result is going to be a flattening of the job candidate landscape. This development was easy to forecast: basically it is the issue of online chess cheating that runs for at least 15 years now.

Hyperpresent

I could pile up similar examples for screens, but I think you've got the idea at this point. I'm going to call this phenomenon the hyperpresent. What is this concept about? It aims at encompassing this trend of seemingly unstoppable convergence in digital societies. Hyperpresent is to digital societies what gravity is to celestial objects. No wonder that stars were the first ones to react to this meta-trend.

Hyperpresent is the result of two digital trends. The first one is the possibility to synchronize using social media without having to rely on a hierarchical structure. The second one is what is called 'digital convergence.' In the virtual world, only 0s and 1s exist. A picture, a video, or a text is ultimately just a large number — namely, the one written in binary in the file recording the thing of interest.

Digital convergence used to be at work when moving from the physical realm toward the virtual realm (e.g., recording a live performance and broadcasting the recording on social networks). Now it is also working the other way around: every move is observed by everyone in real time, and it leads to pandemics of copycat behavior. It is as if this digital convergence phenomenon is fleshed out and takes the form of a massive uniformisation of real-life actions and behaviors.

In this way, our digital life is reminiscent of Kafka's The Trial: what is published on social media is going to be read and used against you by unknown people. Just like K, who is under arrest and put on trial for undisclosed reasons. This uniformity is at the same time accepted — after all, no one is forcing you to use an AI or cosmetic surgery — and imposed: to remain relevant, one cannot do otherwise than follow the crowd. And unlike before, you can't pretend not to know what the crowd is doing.
