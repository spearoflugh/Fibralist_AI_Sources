      The Topological Revolution (sol-002) was developed exclusively through its positive
      dimension: things that become closer (global communication, cross-continental
      communities). This article identifies the forgotten flip side — things that become
      more distant. The nutrition analogy is the formal key: every diet recommendation is
      not only about what you do (eat meat) but about what you stop doing (eat broccoli).
      The treatment effect and the abstention effect are inseparable. On social media: what
      is NOT shown (the 7/8 of your followers who never see your tweet) is numerically more
      important than what IS shown. The default is absence. "You can't talk about what you
      haven't been exposed to." Negative topology: the structurally unperceived becomes
      as causally significant as the perceived.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Sophie's Other Choice — The Forced Epistemic Binary">
      Sophie's Choice (Styron novel) is the paradigm of being forced to choose between two
      unbearable options. "Sophie's Other Choice" is the digital epistemic version: in a
      world of unlimited information, the algorithm forces a choice between two constructed
      realities (pro-Palestinian or pro-Israeli timeline after October 7) — not between bad
      and good options but between two incomplete halves of reality. Unlike Sophie's Choice
      (where the decision was real), this choice is made passively, by clicking/liking/
      following. "Even if you know that the other reality exists you don't live in it."
      The irony: the pre-social-media era had less apparent choice (the NYT decided for you)
      but more shared reality; the social media era has apparent maximum choice but produces
      incompatible reality bubbles.
    </concept>

      "Freedom of speech" debates dominate platform discussions. Sophie's Other Choice
      raises the prior question: freedom of thought. "You think that you are the master

      what you are seeing on social media? And how much of what you see is influenced by
      actors you don't have the slightest idea about?" The troubling asymmetry: in the

      algorithmically imposed. Freedom of speech without freedom of thought is a formal
      right without real content.

I was listening to a French podcast on the communication aspects of the ongoing Israeli-Palestine war in Gaza, "le Collimateur faire la guerre par les images," when I was struck by this idea: there is at the same time less and more communication than before. If there is no real surprise on the "more" side, the "less" side is more insidious and maybe more important.

We are going through what I call a topological revolution. The idea is that our access to information has changed how we perceived the world in terms of distance. This change is very deep: it touches on what you perceive as close/far away from you. Before the massification of new information technologies we had no real choice. Our neighbors were the guys to whom it was possible to speak with. Today anyone on earth can talk to anyone live. My approach to this revolution has been very theoretical and abstract. What the podcast episode made me notice is how I focused only on the new things that are possible to do in this digital era. I had completely forgotten/missed that there is a flip side of the coin. Namely that *things could become more distant than they were before*.

## Nutrition and Opportunities

Nutrition is a weird field of knowledge. There is a deep epistemic problem at the root of the scientific approach to nutrition: when you do an experiment you are manipulating many variables at once. Suppose that you think that red meat is "good for health." Your experiment will consist in feeding group A with red meat and group B with standard diet. When A group only eats meat, it implies that it doesn't eat everything that is not meat. If you find a benefit, maybe the benefit comes from the fact that by eating meat you have stopped eating broccoli which was the source of your health problems. So magically by eating meat you have solved the issues coming from broccoli's consumption. Is it fair to say that meat is good for your health from this experience?

Things are more subtle than that but you get the idea: every diet recommendation is not only about what you do — *it is mostly about what you don't do anymore*.

The same thing is happening on social media. Your X is not my X. The "For you" TL is built by an AI that chooses what you see of your social media circle. I have around 800 followers; a random tweet that I post is displayed 100 times. It means that on average only 1/8 of my followers actually see my tweets. What is not seen is numerically more important than what is seen. This is the default. Of course if you tweet about a hot topic it might influence the algo. Just like in the nutrition example, what does not happen is more important than what actually happens. You can't talk about what you haven't been exposed to.

## Revealed Preferences

Imagine an equivalent of October 7th in the 70s. How would you have learned about it? Presumably through legacy media: either via a newspaper or the TV news show of the night. You wouldn't have had a real choice. The newsroom of the New York Times would have made the choice of the narrative for you, and everyone else in the country. Now flash forward to 2023. You learned about it through social media. And depending on who you follow you might have gotten videos from Hamas/Palestine or from Israel. If the narrative was pretty one-way on October 8th — the attacks were hard to deny — as soon as the Israeli army started to respond you could only look at Palestinian casualties if you were in that circle, and never hear about hostages. Likewise if you were in a pro-Israel environment you had seldom access to videos of the civil casualties caused by the IDF. Very quickly the distance between both realities became impossible to overcome.

Just by clicking, reacting to, liking, you made a choice. This choice led you to see one reality and ignore the other one. Even if you know that the other reality exists you don't live in it. Your TL is not filled with the other side of the story. This is Sophie's Other Choice.

## Freedom of Thought

There are eternal talks around "freedom of speech" and social media platforms. Sophie's Other Choice is about a much more fundamental thing. Freedom of thought is not what we think it is. You think that you are the master of your thoughts, but question yourself: how much of your thoughts is linked to what you are seeing on social media? And how much of what you see is influenced by actors you don't have the slightest idea about? From algorithms to corporate decisions to make some subjects viral or not. The troubling aspect is that it is not clear who does what. At least in the 70s you knew that the editorial room of the NYT was making choices. In the social media era you don't know how much of what you see is determined by your behavior and how much is imposed on you. Like Sophie you are in a position where you can only make a bad decision because you are going to unsee half of the truth.

Welcome to 2024.
