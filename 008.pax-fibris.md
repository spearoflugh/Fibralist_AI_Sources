#Pax Fibris
  
Fibers can develop in parallel, as discussed in “On Bootstrapping Fibralism”. A natural question arises: what happens when two or more parallel instances of the same Fiber reach a dissensus? By what mechanisms is the conflict resolved? In this piece, I am going to sketch some ideas to tackle these issues in the spirit of Fibralism. The bird’s-eye view is as follows: we will draw inspiration from distributed systems and how they reach consensus—a challenge known as the Byzantine Generals Problem, following the seminal article by L. Lamport.
The Byzantine Allegory

A distributed system is a set of computers working together to provide a service. If this definition seems imprecise, it is because even at the research level, there is no universally recognized definition. Unlike a single computer, which has a clear mathematical model—the Turing Machine—distributed systems are harder to pin down.

An illustration may be worth a thousand words: email is a distributed system. You have an idea of what email is: a service used to send and receive messages. However, if you try to think about what email is in concrete terms, it becomes fuzzier. There is no “mega-computer” implementing the email function for the entire world. In fact, I could set up a mail server in a couple of hours on a personal computer. How does this new server change the “email function” at a global level?

Likewise, messages can use several physical artifacts: cables, Starlink satellites, and so on. If I cut a cable, email still works... Thus, email is not something you can “put your hands on,” because there is no particular piece of hardware that is the email. Contrast this with a program running on a single computer.

So, perhaps email is just the SMTP protocol? But a protocol is merely a list of conventions—a piece of paper. And a piece of paper does nothing in itself, right? So what is email, ultimately? As you can see, the answer is much more complex than it first appears. This is primarily because email is a distributed system: essentially, it is a set of computers and programs that agree to follow a script (the protocol), even though no one forces them to do so. My own mail server could follow the rules between 12:00 and 14:00 and ignore them the rest of the day. It would be pointless, but it is possible. Compare this again to a standard program: you have no choice but to use the correct syntax; otherwise, it won’t compile and you won’t have anything at all.

Problems occur when parts of a distributed system are no longer able to communicate with one another—whether because nodes have been compromised, the network has failed, or for any other reason. This leads to parts of the system evolving in parallel, which can result in inconsistencies. Consequently, a major challenge in distributed computing is determining how to re-establish a common state shared by all parts once communication is resumed.

Another issue is linked to the sheer scale of distributed systems: some nodes will inevitably malfunction or “misbehave.” This is the Byzantine fault: how do you ensure the system as a whole functions correctly while certain parts act in a rogue or unpredictable manner?

The allegory provided by Leslie Lamport is as follows:

    Imagine several divisions of the Byzantine army camped outside an enemy city, each commanded by its own general. The generals can communicate only by messenger. After observing the enemy, they must decide upon a common plan of action. However, some of the generals may be traitors, attempting to prevent the loyal generals from reaching an agreement.

    The Byzantines General Problem, Leslie Lamport.

The goal here is not to provide a thorough analysis of this theoretical problem, but to use it as a backbone for our study of what happens when a Fiber instance behaves like a “traitor.” In this context, “traitor” does not imply malice; we are dealing with institutions, not just computers. This refers to situations where conflicts arise between several instances—whether over resources, borders, the legal status of an individual, or other jurisdictional overlaps.
Revisiting classical methods

I cannot remain purely theoretical; otherwise, the text would lack substance and be too ethereal to be of use. Instead, I am focusing on my ideas for a Western Fiber. My first instinct is to examine how civil peace was established in classical Greece—where cities evolved in parallel and were frequently at war—and subsequently in the Roman Empire.

A first method was the Ekecheiria. This was the truce declared during the Olympic Games, allowing participants and spectators to travel through enemy territories with immunity. This truce was respected for over a thousand years, relying on both religious awe and the threat of severe cultural backlash if violated. It served as a localized mechanism to suspend conflict within a shared cultural space: the Hellenic sphere.

Later, the Romans refined this ambition with the Ius Gentium. To settle disputes based on common sense and equity, they prevented conflicts by applying a uniform set of laws across the Empire. This body of law applied to everyone, unlike the Ius Civile, which was reserved exclusively for Roman citizens prior to the Edict of Caracalla.

The Roman solution was fit for a written culture. What we are striving for is a refinement that works for a digital culture. The idea is to get the inspiration from distributed systems. What is looked for is not a set of fixed rules but rather an incentive architecture.
Incentives

Bitcoin is the most famous solution to the Byzantine Agreement Problem. The core challenge is as follows: every node in the network must agree on a single ledger. This ensures that when Alice sends a bitcoin to Bob, the transaction is universally recognized because it is recorded in the ledger, making ownership indisputable.

How does Bitcoin solve this Byzantine problem? Without a central authority, some nodes might attempt to cheat by spending the same bitcoin multiple times—a challenge known as the double-spending problem. While I will not delve into the technical specifics here, the solution involves a mathematical challenge: the node that solves the challenge first earns the right to validate a block of transactions.

Now, why would participants spend vast amounts of energy to solve this challenge? They do so because they are rewarded; validating a block grants them newly minted bitcoins. This is where the Bitcoin network is truly remarkable: it provides a global service without requiring a centralized infrastructure. Participants solve the problem locally, and they do so because the network’s incentive architecture (the reward) aligns their individual interests with the security of the system.

The question to be solved is now: how can we set up an incentive architecture in order for the Fiber to prevent dissensus from happening ?
Proof of materiality

It is nearly impossible to provide precise solutions in a vacuum; they must be designed for real-world systems through a process of trial and error—gradually filtering what works from what does not. My goal here is to offer ideas that are in the spirit of the essence of Fibralism. This leads us to a more refined question: what would be the defining characteristics of an effective incentive architecture? What we do know is that neither the fear of God, Ekecheira, nor the letter of the law, Ius Gentium, are going to make it.

A first step would be to establish a blockchain-based version of the Domesday Book. Belonging to the same Fiber must imply the acceptance of a shared blockchain that records both the status of the Fibrizen and the cadastral data. When different instances of a Fiber merge, they must reach a consensus on this “Domesday blockchain,” which is then synchronized across all instances.

However, this should be viewed only as a starting point. A robust incentive architecture requires more than a static record; it must incorporate dynamic properties. Once again, the Bitcoin example helps illustrate this: the difficulty of the mathematical problem is adjusted according to a fixed rule based on the average number of blocks validated over a specific period. If blocks are validated too quickly, the complexity increases; if too slowly, the challenge is eased.

How does this translate to the political sphere? In Bitcoin, the agreement of the nodes to the validity of the shared ledger is an idea. If it were just an idea nodes would never agree. What makes the protocol to work is the fact that it is anchored in reality. Solving the mathematical challenge demands computation and power at the end of the day. This is going to be the leading principle in looking for solutions that are in the spirit of Fibralism.

Let’s take a concrete example. Suppose that an instance of a Fiber present a person as a Fibrizen but another instance doesn’t agree. Maybe because they want to gain an advantage from recognizing the person as a Fibrizen, be it for economical or political reasons. In such a case the inspection of the Domesday blockchain will be used as a material proof. That example would find a modern application in how immigration law that are decided at the federal level can be challenged at the state level in the USA (see the event around ICE). In a Fiber split the consensus is reached by instances acknowledging the materiality of the proof. Other material proofs can be considered depending on the nature of the dissensus. What are those proofs will be a continual work of definition for Fibers. Just like the legislative corpus evolve in a liberal democracy.

Reality acts as an impartial judge. You can’t cheat with power production. The same must occur in the political sphere. It means that settlement cannot remain in the sphere of justifications. The settlement must include a proof-of-materiality. It is an upgrade to the idea of Bona Fides for Ius Gentium.
