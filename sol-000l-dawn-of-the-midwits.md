We are moving from a written culture towards a digital culture. It has deep implications on the political institutions. I have discussed a lot about that in this substack. But most of the time I have done it from a very theoretical point of view. This tweet made me realize that there are already practical implications.

Actual hierarchy of power in the United States: Harvard/NYT/NGO complex > CIA > Permanent bureaucracy > Corporations > Everyone else — @pegobry

Clearly the powers in the XXI° are not the powers of the XVIII°. Montesquieu was a genius but he couldn't have foreseen the reach of new information technologies. This is what I am trying to investigate in this essay.

## From producer to consumer

> "Practical men who believe themselves to be quite exempt from any intellectual influence, are usually the slaves of some defunct economist" — John Maynard Keynes

Keynes was maybe wrong about many things but he was nonetheless a bright guy. His observation on the fact that practical men are acting on behalf of dead thinkers was spot on. Social media have added this extra feature: nowadays, an intellectual doesn't have to write a book, submit it to a publishing house, react to the editorial changes, correct errors and wait for the publication and broadcast of the physical objects. Instead the intellectual can tweet his ideas before those ideas have been completely formulated. Less than one second after they have been tweeted they are available worldwide. So most probably the author of the idea influencing decision makers is not dead. It may appear without having large consequences. It is not the case.

Legislators are not ethereal subjects. They are humans like you and me. They consume news and are influenced by culture generally speaking. Sure, they are part of a team, and sometimes adhere to some values (who knew?), but this team membership works more like a social media status than a source of inspiration. Because at the end of the day: why do some subjects get talked about in the assemblies rather than others? The power of setting the agenda, meaning "what is talked about", is huge. It used to be the power of the press (the fourth power). It reached its peak during the 70s. With the multiplication of cable news, in the 90s, quickly followed by the mass adoption of internet since the early 2000s, everything changed. Instead of a purely top-down architecture a more complex landscape emerged. People became able to react (web 2.0) and to exchange directly between each other (social media at the age of Facebook and Twitter). The flux of information became more circular, and together with the acceleration of broadcasting it produced a generalized Larsen Effect, aka buzz phenomenon. A poster child example being the swarm that formed, and that pushed politicians to react quickly and aggressively, at the start of the Ukrainian war. More and more leaders are mutating into… followers. They follow the swarm that demands more and more because a swarm is made by individuals that use virtue signaling as currency. The only way to be noticed in the hyper competitive attention market that are social media is to do more, to be the first to etc.

## Reflexivity

Here is the place to cite Keynes a second time in this essay. His idea of the beauty contest to describe price fluctuations in equity markets fits very well with what happens on social media and the new market of ideas. The crux of the issue is that you need to gain traction. So you need to have a lot of followers (or rather influential ones), to have a say on what happens. But forming an audience is not a free process as Gurwinder aptly notices it in "The Perils of Audience Capture". This adds an extra layer of reflexivity. In the realm of social media you observe yourself through the actions of the others (likes, retweets). You react to the social feedback but there is neither filter (aka editorial board) nor a hierarchy. It is very hard to take a step back to look at what unfolds. This can very easily drag you into places you didn't anticipate you were going to. It is a strange kind of self-awareness without full consciousness. Indeed your self-observation is mediated via the reactions of your audience. This audience acts as a filter that is not perceived as such.

A mechanical aspect of digital societies is that because too much information is produced, one has to use statistical tools to navigate the deluge of data. But those statistical tools, by their very nature, produce averages. Everything that is too groundbreaking will be erased. It creates this idea that there is no alternative because the individuals are crushed by the mean value. So the institutions converge towards an eigenvalue that looks like Biden. The caricature couldn't be more pronounced: on one hand you have the pure product of the administration: the essence of a statistical product — notice the etymology of statistics lies in "state", it is not by chance — and on the other hand you have the crazy maverick that has no superego. Because the flip side of this averaging monster is that if you want to be noticed you have to be extreme in the other direction. There is no middle ground left in the digital landscape.

This is the era of the midwits that submerge, by their raw number, the skillful ones. I believe (hope) that this is a transitory moment but I would be hard pressed to find a way out today. The immediate problem is that our institutions are no longer effective at controlling power and this disconnection is noticed by more and more people. Some are going to profit to game the system while others are trying to figure out what new institutions are needed. This announces a bumpy road ahead.
