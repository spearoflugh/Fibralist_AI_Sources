#Fibralism and Borders: A Topological Revolution
Fibralism proposes a new civilizational framework, one that privileges
temporal considerations over spatial ones. Its fundamental aim is the
weaving of a cultural thread capable of spanning generations. This
stands in sharp contrast to the dominant paradigm, which fixates on the
immediate exertion of power through institutions and their territorial
boundaries. This spatial focus gives rise to our central dilemma: how to
manifest power in the physical world? From this question springs the
entire logic of the Westphalian order---the monopoly of violence over a
precisely bounded, contiguous territory.

In Fibralism, the primary objective is to extend a cultural thread
across time. The control of land and state power is secondary---a
necessary means rather than the end itself. Naturally, without some form
of physical grounding, the Fibralist project remains a mere abstract
ideal. History is littered with civilizations that have been physically
erased. Thus, we must address how to establish a tangible real-world
presence from a Fibralist perspective.


##The Topological Revolution

Fibralism is inherently designed for a digital age. In digital cultures,
geographic determinism is far less binding than it was in written or
oral societies. As information and capital flow with unprecedented ease,
we gain a capacity for **synchronization** that simply never existed
before. Historically, the ability to synchronize large populations has
been the state apparatus's most potent lever, allowing it to
consistently outperform any private enterprise.

From this perspective, [new information technologies fundamentally
redistribute
power](https://spearoflugh.substack.com/p/percolation-a-new-model-for-disruption).
Just as firearms democratized violence---allowing an individual with an
AK-47 to rival a much stronger adversary---digital tools flatten the
global power landscape.

The contiguous nation-state was a technical artifact of the written era.
When governance required the physical dispatch of orders from a central
hub, and information moved only at the speed of horse and paper, there
was no viable alternative to territorial contiguity. Digital existence,
however, fundamentally alters this topology. Theoretically, there is no
longer a foundational requirement to control continuous land. The "base
space" of our politics can be reconfigured, even as certain physical
constraints inevitably remain.

##Local Survivability Principle

To endure across generations, a community's cultural fiber must be
anchored in a robust **material foundation**. It must possess the
capacity to secure its fundamental needs: food, water, energy, security,
and communication infrastructure. Lacking this base, any effort to
transmit culture across time remains fragile and purely abstract.

This necessity gives rise to a core Fibralist tenet: the **Local
Survivability Principle (LSP)**. Unlike the classical city-state, which
depends on external hinterlands for its survival, a Fibralist community
strives for strategic independence in these essentials. This does not
demand absolute autarky; rather, it requires the control of---or
resilient access to---sufficient land and resources to withstand
external shocks or blockades. The LSP serves as the indispensable
substrate for the fiber; it is the stable "base space" upon which
long-term cultural weaving depends.

Viewed through the metaphysical lens of the
[Manifesto](https://thefibralist.substack.com/p/the-fibralist-manifesto),
the relationship is clear: **the LSP is to Fibralism what the doctrine
of territorial contiguity is to the nation-state.** Furthermore,
Fibralism diverges from traditional models by ensuring that institutions
themselves are localized, mirroring this material independence.

**Local Institution Principle - Dunbar Hierarchy Levels**
---------------------------------------------------------

An astute reader will note that I have yet to describe the specific
shape of Fibralist institutions. This is intentional: the essence of
Fibralism does not reside in institutional forms. Just as there are no
formal requirements for spatial contiguity, there is no rigid
prescription for how power must be organized. However, Fibralism is far
from a "free-for-all."

Our institutional logic begins with a fundamental anthropological fact:
[Dunbar's Number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dunbar%27s_number). This
theory posits a cognitive limit on the number of stable social
relationships an individual can maintain. Dunbar's informal definition
is evocative: *"the number of people you would not feel embarrassed
about joining uninvited for a drink if you happened to bump into them in
a bar."* For humans, this number is approximately 150.

## The Dunbar Hierarchy Levels (DHL)

Drawing from this trait, we can define the **Dunbar Hierarchy Levels
(DHL)**. Each level represents a scaling of human organization by a
factor of 150:

-   **DHL-0:** 1500=1 --- The individual.

-   **DHL-1:** 1501=150 --- The clan or neighborhood: direct,
    face-to-face relationships.

-   **DHL-2:** 1502=22,500 --- The township or syndicate.

-   **DHL-3:** 1503=3,375,000 --- The metropolis or region.

-   **DHL-4:** 1504=506,250,000 --- The continental fiber or defense
    pact.

-   **DHL-5:** 1505=75,937,500,000 --- The global or interplanetary
    fiber.

In practical terms, five levels are sufficient. Note that DHL-5 exceeds
the current global population, leaving ample room for demographic growth
or expansion.

## Governance as a Protocol

In a Fibralist society, formal structures must exist at each level, yet
there is no requirement for uniformity. One DHL-2 node might operate as
a direct democracy while another adopts a different internal logic.
**Legitimacy flows upward**---from the "Fibrizens" to the institutions,
never the reverse. Consensus operates horizontally within a node and
vertically through formal interfaces.

While these levels are anchored in geography---a small city, for
instance, functions as a DHL-2 structure linked to specific land---the
**digital layer** serves as the medium for synchronization, federation,
and protocol-sharing. It allows a DHL-4 Council to coordinate a
continental defense pact or a DHL-2 Syndicate to trade surplus energy
with a neighbor.

## Key Inquiries & Future Work

As this is a work in progress, several core questions remain:

1.  **Scope and Role:** How is the unity of the Fiber maintained?
    Cultural evolution must be slow enough to ensure continuity. Initial
    proposals include:

    -   **DHL-1:** Neighborhood councils and cultural transmission.

    -   **DHL-2:** Municipal services and local security.

    -   **DHL-3:** Regional coordination and large-scale infrastructure.

    -   **DHL-4:** Defense pacts and high-level diplomacy.

2.  **Stability and Mergers:** How do cells remain bound to the same
    Fiber? When two Fibers merge, the Manifesto suggests the older one
    prevails, but the mechanics of this integration require further
    thought.

3.  **Demographic Shifts:** How does the system respond to declining
    populations in specific geographic nodes?

##Protocol vs. Program

The operating principle of the DHL approach is that of an **open
protocol**, not a centralized program. Contrast this with the
traditional nation-state---such as the French Republic---where a
hierarchical, tree-like structure dictates that *Pr√©fets* execute orders
from Ministers, who answer to a President.

In Fibralism, nothing prevents two DHL-2 communities from cooperating
directly without the mediation of a DHL-3 structure. Higher-order
councils are reserved exclusively for critical, large-scale functions:
civilizational defense, space exploration, or monumental architectural
projects. Under this model, the border ceases to be a hard line on a
map; it becomes a **dynamic set of interfaces**---a living protocol
between survivable nodes.
