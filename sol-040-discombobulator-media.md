An attacker with no positive objective needs only to produce disorder — any error from the defending side is a victory. This fundamental asymmetry favors discombobulation in distributed systems. Social media is uniquely prone to this because it is itself a distributed system optimized for attention, not truth.

This is sonic DDoS applied to human cognition.

"The Discombobulator" is the name given by Trump to a no-longer-secret US Army weapon that was allegedly used to dismantle Maduro's defenses. From the total absence of air defense during the raid to the minimal ground resistance from his soldiers, a whole range of incapacitating technologies appear to have been deployed with great effect. There were zero casualties in the US military. They came, they discombobulated, and they left.

This is an extreme example of a new approach to confrontation — one particularly suited for a digital culture. In the world of social media, information is everything. It stands to reason that disorienting the enemy and muddying the waters is becoming a primary strategy. This is the core subject of this essay: What is discombobulating? Where can it be found? How does it work? What forms does this new way of waging war take? These are the questions we will address here.

But first things first: how did this type of warfare become possible, and why didn't it exist before?

Editing noise out

In a print-based society, the written word is governed by the editorial function. From the laws of the land to the editorial board of the New York Times, and onto scientific journals — the "follow the science" apparatus — there were once processes and structures entirely devoted to the curation of speech. This, by the way, is why the First Amendment is so crucial. In a written culture, the production and broadcasting of information are difficult and, therefore, easier to regulate. A direct result is that noise is filtered out of the global conversation. This is precisely the editor's job: not to censor, but to separate the wheat from the chaff. This is why such a system is extremely difficult to discombobulate.

The internet and social media have changed that. On social media, your double-digit IQ neighbor has a voice. He can tweet, and the entire world can instantly read his thoughts on why the Earth is flat. Of course, the algorithm is there, and its job is more or less that of the editors: to filter out the garbage. But, as we know, humans — even the dim-witted ones — are far smarter than machines. The system is being overrun by both sheer volume and human ingenuity. It has finally become technologically possible to discombobulate it.

Whistle hustle

The recent events in Minnesota involving demonstrators and ICE — specifically the highly contentious deaths of Renee Good and Alex Pretti — illustrate the discombobulating evolution of our societies. Admittedly, these are extreme examples. Yet, as the image of the frog above suggests, they are textbook cases of this new strategic approach to confrontation.

My advice is to watch the raw footage of these two incidents without journalistic commentary or dramatic news music. The soundscape of both — and indeed of most modern demonstrations — is striking: whistles, megaphones, sirens. It sounds like chaos. But it is not accidental; it is engineered chaos. This is the essence of the whistle hustle. There are several points to consider:

First, high-pitched whistles are used to overload the cognitive processing capabilities of ICE agents. Here, a fundamental asymmetry emerges: federal agents are trying to execute a specific task, whereas the anti-ICE protesters are not trying to achieve anything other than pure disorder. Narrative control will follow later on social media. In the moment, whistling doesn't just make the job harder — it makes coordination impossible. It is a simple but vital observation: how can officers coordinate or confirm if an individual is contained if they cannot communicate by voice?

Again, this asymmetry favors the agitators. Their "play" is easier because they lack a specific objective; any error from the opposing side is a victory. Ironically, law enforcement also deployed sonic weapons — officially known as Long Range Acoustic Devices (LRAD) — to disperse the crowds. It is another discombobulating device, though this one is used for a precise, tactical objective.

Rage Bet

The discombobulation of communal life is not restricted to street-level demonstrations; it can be witnessed every day on social media. "Rage bait" has long been viewed as a mere technical trick to capture attention. While true, that is only a micro-level observation. The irritation of social media users has become a global phenomenon. It is not achieved with physical whistles, but with messages and trends crafted to induce cognitive dissonance in the reader.

From Macron wearing impossible-to-miss sunglasses in Davos, to Biden delivering a speech on democracy within a dictatorial staging, or Trump walking with a lone penguin in an Arctic setting — the aim is not to inform or even to disinform. It is to disorient.

The OODA loop concept is often misunderstood; the common belief is that the goal is simply to accelerate your own loop to gain an advantage. Discombobulation, however, is about the destruction of the adversary's capacity to build a functional loop at all. The objective is negative rather than positive. As it turns out, negative objectives are far easier for a distributed organization to produce. This may be why social media — a distributed method of hosting a global conversation — is so uniquely prone to discombobulating tactics.

What a better way to end this piece by quoting this A. Greenspan famous quote? "I guess I should warn you, if I turn out to be particularly clear, you've probably misunderstood what I've said."
