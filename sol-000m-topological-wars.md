Last year I wrote a piece, The Topological Revolution. The idea that I was exploring in this essay was the following one: new information technologies have messed up with our mental landscape. Basically, what is "next" to us is no longer what our senses collect. You are presumably way less integrated within your local community than within your virtual social media circles. Over a year this tendency has not slowed down a bit, on the contrary. Let's explore how things have changed (morphed) during this year of war in Ukraine.

## War with words

Once you start noticing it, it becomes hard to unsee how much topological vocabulary is heavily used in conversations about the news: "Alt-right Adjacent," "First-Amendment-adjacent issues," "Proxy war," "Trumposphere"... The public discourse is full of those words because we are exploratory primates: this is our way to understand the world. In the ancient times, when writing was not yet a thing, people had to store a lot of information in their brains. One technique from antique Greece was to build memory palaces. The idea is to picture yourself a familiar building (or a trip along a path). Then, put objects in the rooms. If you want to recall a list you can put the items of the list, one in each room, and recall them by mentally visiting the rooms. This is the "trick" used by those who remember thousands of digits of Pi. The technique relies on the fact that we are biologically wired to remember places. We remember geographically related things much better than abstract things.

The use of words is of paramount importance in the realm of social media. They shape the discourse. Political decisions are a downstream effect of those conversations. What is talked about in legislative chambers does not fall from the sky. Elected representatives are on Twitter: this is the XXI-century way to perceive the world. By changing the landscape in which the conversation is happening on the public square you can have an enormous influence on the legislative agenda.

## War actions

So this has unravelled, as always, step by step. It started with sanctions, quickly followed by ATGMs and Manpads. Then came big guns, HIMARS, main battle tanks, and before you know it jet fighters are not yet on the table… but you know how it works. The initial statement by the Biden administration was that sending tanks would mean World War III. It took almost a year but here we are.

This gradual progression thing is well-known. If you want to understand how laymen can end up in an Einsatzgruppen, I recommend the book "Ordinary Men" by C. Browning. The recipe can be summarized into the "one step at a time" one liner. The link with topology is the following: the size of the steps is actually changing because of our new information technologies. The way you make people walk to places they didn't want to go in the first place also. For a topologist objects that appear very different are in fact similar — a mug and a doughnut are the same thing from a topological point of view. The idea is that you can apply a series of *continuous deformations* (you don't tear the material or puncture it) and end up with a fundamentally different shape. The continuous deformation is the mathematical equivalent of the "one step at a time" recipe.

It turns out that the public discourse is very malleable. A concrete example is the narrative around the origin of COVID. The last episode is an extraordinary declaration from the Department of Energy: "low confidence" that COVID leaked from a lab. This is continuous deformation being performed under your eyes — new information enters, but yet it remains "low confidence." The narrative clearly evolves while maintaining plausible deniability. Remember that many professional journalists were sacked just for asking questions about it.

## Wars are just starting

Firstly, our mental topology itself is changing. It is hard to grasp because topology is what allows you to have a discussion about shapes. The very ways by which we understand notions like "neighborhood relation" is evolving. It is a higher-order transformation.

Secondly, we have to develop/invent an equivalent of topological characteristics that makes the manipulation of the global conversation manifest. The evolution of the narrative around COVID is just one example. We should not accept such evolutions without requiring solid explanations and a reckoning. What has been done is similar to puncturing the surface in topology: it is not a transformation that is accepted without serious justification. Have you heard any institution saying something like "sorry we fucked up, and here is why we fucked up, and here is how we are not going to fuck up again next time"?

Me neither.

The discrepancies between what is said and reality seem to get larger by the day. In a distributed system, losing one election is at worst like a node that is compromised. The system is going to continue to work as if nothing happened — just as the Bitcoin network quickly recovered from being banned by China. This is the Byzantine fault problem: up to 1/3 of failing nodes, the system continues to work. It surely doesn't translate directly for political institutions but something along those lines might be. I don't expect that the Milei experiment will bring a lot for this reason. At best he is one failing node.

The Möbius strip: the internet social media environment is strange because there is no longer "behind the scene." Everything appears as being visible — there is only one side of the strip. Yet there are invisible forces at play. And in such an environment, elections appear more and more irrelevant. The hard point is that it is not clear where power really resides today — in historical institutions? In corporations? In their relationship? It bears more than superficial resemblance with distributed systems, in which it is hard to pinpoint where the system is. More on this soon.
