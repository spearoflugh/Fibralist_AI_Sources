I was shocked by this David Shipley opinion piece in the Telegraph titled “ Patriots should not fight for the British State ”. I was shocked because it encapsulated in a single sentence a subject I have been reflecting on for some time. This article made obviously clear things that I was trying to formalize in Polanyi and Chesterton are in a Field . I recommend reading it to understand the context surrounding this essay. I am trying to always write self-contained essays though. The opposition between “Patriots” and “British State” is the best encapsulation of this large trend of institutional decay due to the digital revolution. Patriots should not fight for the British state The Nation State Divorce The "Nation-State" is a relatively new concept in the span of human history. It became the norm following the bloody Thirty Years' War, which culminated in the Westphalian system of 1648. The most striking evidence that nations and states are not equivalent can be found in extreme cases: The Vatican is a state without nation. Aside from the Borgia family —Pope from father to daughter— there is no people from the Vatican. The Kurds are a nation without a state. They represent a coherent cultural group scattered across Turkey, Irak, Syria, Iran. In a nutshell: the State is an administrative enterprise managing "legitimate violence," while the Nation is the organic, cultural gathering of a people. The two are not strangers: a shared culture fosters acceptance of institutional authority, and the state can help forge a nation (for instance, by imposing a common language). The State is the wrapper; the Nation is the evanescent gift. Why evanescent? Because unlike the State you can’t really grasp the Nation. While the State has tangible expressions —like institutional buildings, armed forces, ways to recover the taxes — it is much harder to define a Nation. In computer science terms the State is like a single program on a computer while the Nation is rather like a protocol. A program must obey a precise syntax to be compiled and executed, but no one "enforces" a protocol in the same way. Think of it as a distributed system (like email) versus software (like Word) installed on a personal computer. The Telegraph opinion piece makes the divorce between the State and the Nation painfully clear: The nation has changed almost beyond recognition since we were last called upon to mobilise and fight a global war. We are no longer one people, but numerous parallel societies with little to no connection to one another. Presiding over this is an incompetent bureaucracy wedded to universalist ideas and chiefly concerned with its own survival. Talk about a death certificate of the multiculturalist project. It is like a forensic pathologist report. Ok we know that the patient died from a heart attack. What is not appearing in the forensic’s evaluation is that the patient was morbidly obese, drinking a gallon of whisky a week and a chain smoker too. Strictly speaking, he died of a heart attack; that would satisfy the fact checkers of the world. Meanwhile in France the concert of the new year’s eve in Paris , in front of the Arc de Triomphe, is cancelled due to terrorist threats. This follows the near-cancellation of the July 14th fireworks in Lyon , reportedly for financial reasons. The State is in such a bad shape that it can’t provide its flagship product, security (be it financial security or the civil peace), for the Nation to exist. Why be surprised by Shipley’s formula? People can no longer gather. The "why" is almost secondary; if the State excels at anything, it is finding excuses, declaring emergencies, and muddying the waters. Escape Velocity A Nation is hard to pin down with words. The Nation is a shared feeling of belonging. It cannot be measured like the gravitational constant. It changes slowly, measured in generations rather than news cycles. Yet, it exists. Conversely, the state changes at the speed of digital technology. The state is more susceptible to technological disruption than the nation. Borders, for instance, are becoming fuzzy. This is the reality behind "electoral interference"—speech is no longer local or controllable—and the permanent immigration crisis. Technology allows immigrants to remain tethered to their home villages through video calls. Borders are not peripheral (that sounds paradoxical but the borders are central for the State) to the State; the IRS collects taxes right up to the line where another state begins. But what happens when wealth is a Bitcoin wallet? Which side of the border is it on? How does the administration even know it exists? How long can a stationary object be paired with a moving one? It is hard to pinpoint the exact moment they divorced—it’s the Sorites paradox (at what point does a grain of rice turn a heap into a stack?) or a version of the Ship of Theseus . Since WWII, states have drifted, occupying more space and gradually stifling the nation. The COVID crisis is a credible candidate for the "breaking point." The fact that half the world was placed under house arrest for largely questionable reasons will not soon be forgotten. During this crisis, the State effectively halted the Nation. People were forbidden to gather. The world stopped, yet the State continued to churn out regulations and "stretchy" emergency powers. The allegory is so blatant one can only smile at the irony: it wasn't subtle, but it was effective. Elaborated vs simple Kiss is good. KISS — Keep it simple stupid. We have forgotten about that deep truth in our so called post modern societies. As the abstract is what is most easily written, and as constant communications took over reality with the spread of smartphones, we have forgotten to touch grass between notifications. There is this Moravec’s paradox : it is comparatively easy to make computers exhibit adult level performance on intelligence tests or playing checkers, and difficult or impossible to give them the skills of a one-year-old when it comes to perception and mobility. Moravec, Hans (1988), Mind Children. Our society is being reshaped by these technologies. AIs use words to simulate feelings, whereas humans start with feelings and try to convert them into words. The State and the Nation are starting from two different places. Landing A paratrooper touching grass after floating in the air. Right now we are alike a paratrooper who has just jumped from the airplane. The speed is intoxicating —obituaries written by AIs, convenient isn’t it—, fresh air on the face —the possibilities of the tech appears boundless, UBI is just around the corner—. But the ground is always there, waiting — the TFR is collapsing across the globe — and it is approaching fast — institutions have lost legitimacy everywhere in the West —. Will the parachute open? Will the paratrooper touch grass with his hand after landing? Are there any snakes hiding?
