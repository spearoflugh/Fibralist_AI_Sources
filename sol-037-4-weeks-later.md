    <concept name="The 4-Week Test">
      Try to recall a conversation you had with an AI four weeks ago without searching your chat history. The answer is almost always: nothing retained. By contrast, knowledge acquired through struggle — hiking vs cable car to the mountain summit — transforms the knower. AI-mediated knowledge leaves the view unchanged but the viewer unchanged too. The 4-week test is the empirical proof of the Second Law of Metaphysics (sol-034): without the cost of acquisition, knowledge does not stick.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Tainted Blood Drop / Rage Contagion">
      By analogy with 28 Days Later: social media rage-bait infects through a single drop — one image, one headline — not through sustained exposure. The infection is nearly instantaneous and rewires the brain. Unlike the film character who knows he is turning and can say goodbye, younger generations raised entirely inside the digital ecosystem have no prior baseline to notice the change. The pre-digital generation is the last to have the reference point.
    </concept>

Pre-digital people, with their memories of serendipity, physical presence, and non-algorithmic sociality, may become "legends" — mythical creatures of a prior era — to generations who have never known anything else. The question is not whether digital culture is worse, but whether there remains anyone with a reference point to compare.
    </concept>

Danny Boyle's 28 Days Later deserves its status as a cult classic. Shot in 2001 using digital cameras, it was a rarity for a mainstream production at the time, arriving right at the onset of the digital revolution. Precisely around this time, the Internet was beginning to massively enter homes across the globe. Today, the movie can be reinterpreted in light of this unfolding digital revolution.

To refresh the reader's memory, or to provide context for those unfamiliar with the film, here is a brief summary of the plot: After a highly contagious "Rage" virus decimates Great Britain, a courier named Jim wakes from a coma to find London deserted and society collapsed. He bands together with a small group of survivors to travel across the country toward a promised military sanctuary near Manchester. However, upon arrival, the group discovers that the uninfected soldiers guarding the refuge pose a threat at least as sinister as the infected hordes they fled.

24 Years Later

It has now been 24 years — a year has passed for every hour in a day since the film's was shot. I propose a thought experiment: Imagine you are Cillian Murphy's character, Jim. You wake up from a 24-year coma and attempt to decipher this new world.

Superficially, the buildings and cars look the same as the era you left. But the invisible fabric of life has shifted. You have no smartphone. On the subway, nearly 100% of your fellow travelers are locked into their screens, earbuds in, oblivious to the world. The zombie allegory feels strikingly appropriate. Books have vanished from public view.

Even deeper, the digital revolution has rewired how human connections are formed. Consider how couples meet. The shift between 2001 and 2025 is stark: social circles have virtualized (we used to meet partners through friends; now, we meet through algorithms); the office romance is dying (workplaces once generated about 20% of all couples, that figure has been cut in half); ambiguity is gone (today, when people meet, the intent is pre-defined — there is a specific market for everything: romance, casual encounters, or senior dating, serendipity has been engineered out of the process).

In the meantime the total fertility rate plummeted across the world. The TFR of a demographic giant like India moved from 3.1 to 1.94. Globally the Sub-Saharan Africa part of the world remains the only one above the replacement rate today. If the digital revolution is certainly not the only reason, it can't be totally devoid of influence regarding this global trend either.

The 4 weeks test

For those of us "zombies" locked into social media and other attention-grabbing apps, it is incredibly difficult to notice how quickly we are unlearning things. While AI has made access to knowledge easier than ever, it has also made it easier than ever to forget what we have just "learned."

Consider the "four-week trial": try to remember a conversation you had with an AI four weeks ago. Without searching through your chat history, what do you actually retain? If you are like me, the answer is likely "not much." This isn't surprising. The view from a mountain peak isn't the same if you reached the top by cable car rather than by hiking. The view is identical, but the viewer is not the same. Something similar happens when we learn through the "help" of AI.

A similar observation can be made regarding news and social media. You likely gather more raw data by scrolling for thirty minutes than by listening to a traditional TV anchor. However, volume is not the only metric. A social media feed is a deconstructed patchwork of formatted messages — a far cry from the carefully constructed narrative of a skilled journalist. The fact that the journalistic class has often abused this responsibility by pushing false narratives — from the Iraq War to the heights of COVID — does not change our fundamental human need to organize information into stories.

The digital revolution has changed us. The transformation is sharp and visible: much like the infected in the movie, we are becoming more susceptible to rage, and division is sweeping through society at an unprecedented rate.

Tainted Blood Drop

There is a pivotal scene in the movie where a character contracts the disease not by being "half-eaten" in the usual zombie fashion, but through a single drop of blood falling from a suspended corpse. The drop lands directly in his eye, which turns from blue to red at a breathtaking speed — the hallmark sign of the infection.

It is hard not to see two allegories for our times here: social media engagement tactics and "rage bait" (symbolized by the crow and the urge to react to it). The scene speaks for itself. The infection isn't a slow process; it is an almost instantaneous, ocular intrusion that rewires the brain.

There is one element of that scene, however, that differs from our current reality. The character is aware that he is about to turn; he has the chance to say a few kind last words to his daughter before being overcome by the Rage virus. I am not so sure that future generations will have such a luxury.

After all, I was raised before the Internet. As a former computer science student, I first accessed the network in 1992. I am a dinosaur — but a dinosaur who has seen this entire world unfold before his eyes, and from the inside out. I have watched the nature of online conversation sour in real-time. But what about the generations raised entirely within this ecosystem? Is the "Nick Fuentes phenomenon" the new baseline? Is something even more extreme waiting for us in the near future?

I Am Legend

I Am Legend is another zombie film, based on the novel of the same name by Richard Matheson. "Based on" is the right term, because the movie bears little resemblance to the book. Spoiler alert for those who haven't read or seen it: the ending of the film is the exact opposite of the novel.

In the Hollywood version, Will Smith becomes the hero by discovering a cure. In the book, however, the protagonist spends his days hunting the creatures, only to realize that they are not mindless — and that they are terrified of him. Because they cannot withstand the sun, he uses the daylight as a "superpower" to slay them in their sleep. To them, he is the monster; he has become a nightmare figure whispered about in their new society. He has become a legend.

Are pre-digital people destined to become "legends" in this same way? Am I Legend?
