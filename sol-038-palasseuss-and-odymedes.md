    <concept name="Myth as Epistemic Forcing Function">
      Applying a mythological framework to current political figures forces the reader to step outside their social media bubble — the myth operates as a Trojan horse to penetrate the narrative walls of the TL. Unlike direct political analysis (which activates partisan defenses), mythological mapping is emotionally neutral enough to permit genuine perspective-shifting. The myth does not argue; it reveals.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Two Valid Contradictory Mappings">
      The same political figures (Trump, Vance, Rubio) can be mapped onto the same mythological archetypes (Achilles, Odysseus, Palamedes) in two equally coherent configurations that produce contradictory conclusions. This is not a failure of the method — it is its most important result: it demonstrates empirically that the same social media reality produces two non-reconcilable movies for different audiences. The problem is not which mapping is correct; the problem is that both are equally supported by available data.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Algorithm as Perspective Remover">
      In the Iliad, key moments occur when heroes shift perspective — Odysseus meets Achilles in the underworld and learns that the ultimate warrior would choose life over glory. Social media algorithms suppress precisely these perspective-shifting encounters. The Trojan horse of mythological analysis attempts to restore what the algorithm removes: the moment of genuine cognitive dissonance that changes the viewer.
    </concept>
    <concept name="Palamedes Always Dies">
      Across both mappings, one conclusion holds: Palamedes (the virtuous intellectual who exposed the pretense) ends up eliminated. Whether Vance or Rubio plays Palamedes, the result is the same. Being right and virtuous does not protect you. This is not pessimism — it is a structural observation about power dynamics in digital-era politics that transcends which mapping is correct.
    </concept>

<content>
The more time passes, the more it appears that we are once again replaying a new version of the mythological stories that founded the West. Trump resembles Achilles in his seemingly unstoppable force. He has already slain Hector — any hint of decency in politics at least since he ran for the first term starting in 2015 — and Greenland might prove to be his unsuspecting weakness. If we accept these parallels, for what it's worth, the natural question becomes: who or what will play the role of Odysseus or Palamedes? This question may be at the origin of surprising answers linked to our digital culture.

Palasseuss and Odymedes

JD Vance-as-Odysseus pick is compelling at many levels: The rhetorical skill — JD is a mesmerizing speaker when he wants to be, he has the "words like winter snows" quality of the Iliad, he can easily shift registers from intellectual Yale Law graduate to Appalachian everyman. The isolationism — Odysseus fundamentally wants to go home, to withdraw from grand adventures and foreign entanglements, Vance's foreign policy instincts align with this. The pragmatic survivor — Vance has shown remarkable adaptability, from never Trumper to Trump's most loyal defender, like Odysseus feigning madness to avoid war, Vance knows when to bend, when to transform himself for survival.

That would make Marco Rubio as Palamedes. It works perfectly too: The exposer of pretense — Rubio was one of Trump's most fierce critics in 2016, calling out the performance, the con, like Palamedes exposing Odysseus's feigned madness. The subsequent fate — Odysseus never forgives Palamedes, he eventually destroys him by planting gold in his tent and forged letters to make him appear a traitor, Rubio after his 2016 exposure attempt has been systematically diminished, from "Little Marco" to supplicant, now Secretary of State but clearly subordinate. The intelligent rival — Palamedes was considered Odysseus's intellectual equal, which is precisely why Odysseus felt threatened. The tragic irony — Palamedes is a figure of undeserved punishment, punished for being right, for seeing clearly.

This parallels work very well, but wait... Here's another movie.

In fact, it is JD that is Palamedes: The initial exposer — Vance was one of Trump's most scathing critics ("America's Hitler"), like Palamedes exposing Odysseus's feigned madness. The virtuous intellectual — Vance presents himself as a man of principles, the author of Hillbilly Elegy, the one who "truly" understands forgotten America. The vulnerability — despite his position as Vice President, Vance remains strangely vulnerable, not loved, not completely trusted by Trump.

That would make Rubio as Odysseus: The ultimate survivor — Rubio survived "Little Marco," bent without breaking, accepted humiliation to stay in the game, this is exactly Odysseus's patience. The pragmatist without illusions — Rubio never pretended to superior moral virtue, he plays the power game with brutal realism. The Secretary of State — perfect position for Odysseus, the one who navigates between dangers, who speaks with foreigners, who manages crises with cunning rather than force.

A single reality, two movies

What I have just done is what you are experiencing everyday on social media. For the same event, many readings are possible. Last week's Minnesota fatal shooting of Renee Good involving an ICE agent is a textbook example. It illustrates how two completely different America are sharing the same reality.

In one reality Renee Good was just here dropping her kid off at school and acting as a legal observer. She was afraid when seeing two masked men approaching her vehicle and panicked. She was fleeing when shot.

The other reality saw Renee Good as an activist intentionally looking to create an incident. She was acting under the orders of her lover and tried to harm a federal officer on duty.

Depending on your profile, you may have experienced mostly the first or the second narrative on your social media timeline. At this point, it is impossible to know more about the elusive truth. Moreover, I am living thousands of miles away. It is not like I can witness the facts with my bare eyes. I will have, at best, second- or third-hand testimonials: videos stripped of context, AI-doctored and pre-thought analyses — in both movies, there were people and robots locked and loaded with ready-to-serve talking points.

Trojan horse

The myth acts as a revealing agent. It can be seen as a Trojan horse in itself. It is a trick that I used to force you to think differently behind the walls of your social media profile.

In the myth, there are key moments where heroes shift perspectives. This is what social media algorithms take from you. Odysseus meets Achilles in the underworld. Achilles tells Odysseus: "I'd rather be a slave to the poorest man on earth than king of all the dead." The ultimate warrior, given the choice again, would choose life over glory. This fundamentally challenges everything Odysseus believed about heroic values. The meeting with his mother also shifts him. He learns of the suffering his absence has caused and sees the cost of his choices.

Of all the uncertainties, one thing is certain in the myth, though: Palamedes ends up dying. Being right and virtuous doesn't necessarily save you.
